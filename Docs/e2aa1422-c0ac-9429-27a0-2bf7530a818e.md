# Condensation

## What causes condensation?

Condensation often occurs during colder weather when moist indoor air contacts cooler surfaces. This causes moisture to condense into water droplets or frost. The risk increases with colder weather and higher indoor humidity.

For windows, doors, and skylights, condensation can form:
- on the **glass**
- on the **frames and sashes**
- near the **weather seals**
- on the **walls around a skylight**
- between the **glass panes** _(if the seal is broken, the insulating glass unit needs replacement)_

## Reducing condensation

### 1. Buy high performance ENERGY STAR certified windows, doors and skylights.

These products are **more energy efficient** and experience **less condensation** compared to ordinary models.

**Features contributing to better energy efficiency are:**
- **Triple glazing**
- **Low-emissivity (low-e) coatings** on the glass
- **Inert gas** between the glass panes
- **Thermally improved spacer bars**
- **Insulated frames, sashes and doors**
- **Greater air tightness**

_**Note:** Low-e coatings reduce heat loss, but if the night is cool, calm, and humid, condensation may form on the **outside surface of the glass** around sunrise. There's also a higher risk if the low-e coating is on the room-side surface._

### 2. Ensure proper air circulation near windows.

Good airflow keeps glass surfaces warmer. This is why heat sources like central heating vents and electric baseboards are often located beneath windows. Obstructions like **deep window sills, closed drapes and blinds, or nearby furniture** can block air movement, allowing cool air to pool and lead to condensation.

### 3. Make sure your Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV) is on.
