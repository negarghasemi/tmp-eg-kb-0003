# Keeping The Heat In - Section 7: Insulating Walls: insulating, renovating and building additions

Walls can account for about **20 percent of heat loss** in houses. In addition to heat loss through the walls, there are many cracks and penetrations that allow uncontrolled air leakage into and out of the house. This section provides information about wall types, insulation methods, and renovation considerations to maximize energy efficiency.

### Types of wall construction

There are three main types of wall construction used in Canadian houses:

*   **i) Solid walls**
    *   Built of brick, stone, concrete block, log, or wood plank.
    *   Do not have a cavity that can be insulated; insulation must be added to the exterior or interior.
    *   Many have a small drainage cavity (less than 25 mm / 1 in.) that should **never be insulated** or have its drain holes plugged.
    *   _Figure 7-1: Double brick construction (Cutaway of brick interior and exterior walls, held together with brick ties and separated by an air space. The interior face is covered by a layer of strapping, then a lath and plaster finish.)_
    *   _Figure 7-2: Wood plank construction (Cutaway of a wood plank exterior wall with horizontal planks one on top of the other. Diagonal strapping covers the exterior face of plank wall and a layer of tar paper lies between it and the exterior finish. On the interior, lath and plaster covers the planks.)_

*   **ii) Concrete block**
    *   Usually have hollow cores, which allow air circulation and increase convective heat losses.
    *   Insulating cores offers minimal thermal resistance due to thermal bridges from block's internal webs and mortar.
    *   **Recommendation**: Seal all possible air leakage routes into the block wall and ensure the top course of blocks is capped.
    *   Where block walls form party walls, ensure the space between the block wall and interior finish is fully air sealed.
    *   _Figure 7-3: Concrete block construction, a party wall with an open wall (Cutaway of two units, A and B, that share a concrete block wall. The block wall is capped and filled. The open spaces between the interior stud-frame walls of each unit and the common wall are sealed at the top with caulking.)_

*   **iii) Frame walls**
    *   Have a cavity that may be insulated.
    *   Construction techniques determine cavity size and access.
    *   Internal elements (top/bottom plates, fire stops, blocking, plumbing, wiring, heating ducts) can interfere with insulation.
    *   A frame house with brick veneer usually has a 25 to 50 mm (1 to 2 in.) air space for drainage; **do not insulate this space**.
    *   If a drainage plane air space exists, it's better to add insulation directly to the exterior of the frame wall, then incorporate appropriate air barrier, drainage plane, and siding.
    *   _Figure 7-4: Potential blockages in the wall, including wires, pipes, blocking, windows and doors (Illustration of an exterior facing wall with a window. The stud framing is exposed, showing wiring and wood blocking, and the wall is partially covered by exterior siding.)_

### Opportunities for upgrading

*   Using **dense-pack insulation techniques**, empty and partially insulated cavity frame walls can be insulated from the top, bottom, interior, or exterior.
*   Insulating should include **air sealing**.
*   Walls can be insulated as part of major repair or renovation (e.g., interior wall repairs, electrical upgrades, re-siding).
*   **Address any moisture or structural problems** (staining, mould, rot, cracks, misaligned windows/doors) before insulating.
*   Consider both **vapour barriers and air barriers**, especially when adding insulation to existing walls. The vapour barrier must be on the warm third of the finished wall.

## 7.1 Blown-in insulation

*   Professional insulation contractors can blow in loose-fill insulation or inject approved foam insulation into wood-frame wall cavities.
*   **Dense-pack techniques** with cellulose, glass fibre, and approved foams will compress existing batt insulation and fill voids, offering good air sealing.
*   Thermal resistance gains may be limited if the wall already has some insulation, but good results are achievable.
*   **Method**: Small holes (15 to 50 mm /  to 2 in.) are drilled into each stud space. A long tube is inserted and withdrawn in stages to fill the cavity to the proper density.
*   **Product Selection**: Review characteristics of blown-in and foamed-in insulations.
*   **Contractor Collaboration**: Consult with a contractor, specify manufacturer's recommended densities, and agree on the number of bags/containers to be used. Too little can leave voids; too much can cause bulging.
*   **Hole Sealing**: Verify how holes will be sealed, patched, and finished.

### Installing the insulation

There are three ways to install blown-in insulation:

*   **i) From the inside**
    *   Drill small holes through the inside wall finish.
    *   Blow or inject insulation directly into the wall.
    *   Completely plug and seal holes.
    *   If replacing or re-covering interior finish: drill holes, blow insulation, install a well-sealed air and vapour barrier over the old wall, apply new drywall and finish.

*   **ii) From the outside**
    *   Most exterior siding can be drilled, lifted, or removed.
    *   Brick siding can have single bricks temporarily removed to access sheathing.
    *   **Do not insulate the drainage cavity** between brick veneer and stud walls.
    *   Ensure installer patches holes section by section to avoid water entry.

*   **iii) From the basement/attic**
    *   Easiest if access is available and cavity is open top-to-bottom (e.g., balloon frame construction).
    *   All stud spaces need to be filled, allowing for obstructions (windows, doors, fire stops, cross braces).
    *   Contractor determines if filling from top, bottom, or both.
    *   Ensure access holes are tightly sealed.

## 7.2 Renovating the interior

If planning extensive interior renovation, you have two options:

### 7.2.1 Rebuild the existing wall

*   Remove existing wall board or plaster from a wood frame wall.
*   Add or upgrade existing wall cavity insulation.
*   Install vapour barrier, new drywall, and finish.
*   **Improvement**: Reduce thermal bridging by adding 50 mm x 50 mm (2 in. x 2 in.), 50 mm x 76 mm (2 in. x 3 in.), or 50 mm x 89 mm (2 in. x 4 in.) horizontal strapping and filling with insulation.
*   **Vapour Barrier**: Add on the warm side of the wall, within the first third of the overall wall insulation's thermal resistance value (1/3  2/3 rule).
    *   _Figure 7-5: Retrofitting the existing wall and applying the two thirds rule (Illustration of a wood-frame exterior above-grade wall showing the steps for insulating from the interior which include: 1) insulating the old wall, 2) extending the electrical boxes, 3) installing or repairing air barrier, 4) adding perpendicular strapping, 5) horizontal insulation between the strapping, 6) the option of extending the insulation past the partition walls, and 7) applying the new vapour barrier.)_
*   **Rigid Foam Board**: Alternatively, screw rigid foam board directly to exposed studs after filling cavity. Foam board offers high RSI (R) value, saves interior space, and provides a thermal break. Seal foam board as a vapour barrier or install an approved one.
*   **Spray Foam**: If professionally installed:
    *   **Low-density**: Remove existing cavity insulation (or strap wall for deeper space), then fill. Low-density foam acts as an air barrier, but requires an additional vapour barrier.
    *   **High-density**: Strap the wall to create a deeper cavity. Spray a continuous layer (minimum 50 mm / 2 in.) against exterior sheathing and framing members. Fill remaining cavity with other insulation. High-density spray foam acts as a vapour barrier, so no polyethylene vapour barrier is needed. Install a smart barrier or use the ADA method before finishing.

### 7.2.2 Build a new wall on the inside of an existing one

*   Build a new insulated wall onto existing wood frame or masonry walls.
*   New frame wall can be installed any distance from the old wall, provided all cavities are filled with insulation.
*   If a new, well-sealed vapour barrier is installed, there's no need to remove or damage the old one.
    *   _Figure 7-6: Building a new wall on the interior of an existing wall (Cutaway of a new interior facing wall built in front of an existing exterior wall. The new wall framing is attached to the old wall. The window frame is extended to meet the new frame. Batt insulation completely fills the spaces between the vertical studs. A continuous air and vapour barrier covers the wall is sealed with caulking at the top, bottom and any overlaps, and covered with drywall. Alternately, an airtight drywall approach or A.D.A. finish is used.)_
*   Follow instructions for insulating a basement from the inside (sealing air leaks, framing, insulating in two layers, installing air/vapour barrier, applying finish).

**Additional points**

*   When installing air and vapour barriers, cover the entire wall area, including window and door openings. Seal all joints, openings, and penetrations.
*   Plan for insulation to extend behind obstructions (pipes, electrical boxes, heating ducts) so they are on the warm side. Move electrical boxes into the new wall and install in poly pans sealed to the barrier. Insulate plumbing pipes only from behind to prevent freezing. Air seal all air duct joints.
*   Seal cracks around door or window frames with non-expanding polyurethane foam sealant or insulation/backer rod and caulk.
    *   _Figure 7-7: Low expansion foam insulation can seal gaps around window and door frames (Illustration of a canister spraying low-expansion foam insulation in gaps between a window frame and an insulated exterior wall.)_
*   Consider extending insulation past junctions at partition walls by removing drywall, cutting back the partition, extending insulation, and sealing the air/vapour barrier.
*   For a fast option to increase RSI and reduce thermal bridging, install at least **RSI 0.88 to 1.76 (R-5 to 10)** rigid board insulation directly to the wall finish and cover with drywall. Ensure vapour barrier is sealed and reposition electrical components. Consider blown-in insulation in wall cavities simultaneously.

## 7.3 Renovating the exterior

*   **Best Practice**: Remove existing siding, perform structural/utility upgrades, then install additional insulation under new siding.
*   Rigid board or batt/blanket insulation can be applied over old siding.
*   _Figure 7-8: Adding insulation to the exterior (Illustration of a corner of a one storey home with horizontal siding, a slanted roof and a window in one wall. The walls are framed out with new studs, and studs extend the window openings. The space between the studs is filled with rigid insulation over the existing wall. The insulation and new siding are separated by a layer of building paper or house wrap.)_

**Points to keep in mind:**

*   **Insulation Amount**: Significant amounts can be added as space is usually not a limitation, except for lot-line restrictions or limited roof overhangs.
*   **Extensions**: Allow for extending window/door jambs and other penetrations (vents, electrical service, pipes, eavestroughs).
*   **Rim Joist**: Extend new insulated wall past the rim joist area to seal air leakage. Extend to at least **200 mm (8 in.)** above grade, and if basement is uninsulated, extend a minimum of **600 mm (24 in.)** below ground level over the foundation wall.
*   **Cost**: Material cost of exterior insulation is typically **15% to 20%** of the total retrofit cost. Consider adding higher levels (e.g., **R-15 to R-20** instead of R-4 to R-10).
*   **Vapour Barrier Location**: If new insulation has at least twice the insulating value of the old wall, a new vapour barrier can be installed over the old wall. This is common for solid masonry walls, using polyethylene sheet or high-density spray foam.
*   **Air Barrier**: Alternatively, install a new air barrier (e.g., spun polyolefin) on the exterior and leave the existing vapour barrier inside. Seal all penetrations, seams, joints, and perimeter edges. If extending below the rim joist, seal and mechanically fix to the foundation.
*   **Air Space**: Limit any air space between existing wall and new construction to no more than **10 mm (3/8 in.)**. This can help moisture escape but reduces insulation effect by about 5 percent.
*   **Exterior Framing**: Generally requires a top plate along the soffit and a bottom plate/box beam attached to the foundation wall.
    *   _Figure 7-9: The box beam can overlap at corners (Cutaway of an exterior corner of a home with insulation and new studs installed on top of the existing siding. A box beam attached to the base of the above-ground exterior wall supports the new framing. The box beam is shown extending out at a corner to create an overlap with the box beam of the other wall. A weather or air barrier covers the original wall. The new framing is supported by the box beam and is spaced out from the existing wall to allow for a horizontally-installed layer of insulation. A second layer of batt insulation is placed vertically between the studs.)_
*   Ensure air barrier at the top is sealed to the old wall, and adequate roof ventilation is maintained.
*   Ensure eaves prevent wind-blown water from entering between insulation and siding; add flashing and caulk top joints if necessary.

### Rigid board insulation

*   Any type can be used, but check local building codes for minimum RSI values, air barrier, and vapour barrier requirements.
*   Impermeable foam board with a true **RSI value of 1.14/25 mm (R-6.5/in.)** is generally considered good.
*   Fasten rigid board with appropriate fasteners.
*   Ensure framing and insulation fit snugly without gaps.
*   Check siding manufacturer's recommendations for fasteners on top of rigid board.
*   **Insulated siding**: Offers low insulating value (**RSI 0.35 to 0.70 (R-2 to 4)**). Should incorporate a drainage feature; if not, add a drainage plane.
*   **Vertical/Diagonal Strapping**: Provides drainage plane, reduces heat buildup, and offers nailing surface for siding while reducing thermal bridging.

### Batt/blanket insulation

*   Build a wooden framework on top of the outside wall to hold insulation and support siding. Ensure air and vapour barriers are properly installed.
*   Alternatively, a lightweight frame wall (e.g., Larsen truss) can be hung from rafters or supported on a bottom plate. This allows two layers of batt insulation (one horizontal, one vertical), achieving **RSI 3.5 (R-20)** or higher.
    *   _Figure 7-10: Trusses can be hung from the rafters and nailed to the existing wall (Cutaway of the top of an exterior wall and a slanted roof, Trusses are attached to the rafters of the roof overhang, as well as the existing wall. Vertical batt insulation is placed between the trusses.)_

## 7.4 Miscellaneous spaces: attached garages and more

Insulate and air seal walls and floors separating heated from unheated spaces.

*   **Attached unheated garage**
    *   Walls, ceiling, and door adjoining the house must be insulated and air sealed to reduce heat loss and prevent fumes from entering the house.
    *   For walls, refer to sections on blown-in or interior renovation (above ground) or basement insulation (below ground).
    *   If garage ceiling is open and joists are visible, proceed as for open foundations.
    *   Remove existing garage ceiling finish to properly air seal and insulate.
    *   If finished ceiling exists, dense-pack insulation can be blown into the space between it and the floor above. Carefully re-seal all holes to prevent fumes from leaking.
    *   **Safety warning**: Never run heating supply and return ducts from the house into a garage due to danger and building code violations.
    *   Professionally installed spray foam against the bottom of the floor above is recommended if no ceiling finish, as it provides vapour barrier, air barrier, and initial insulation layer. Fill remaining space with additional insulation. Exposed spray foam must be covered with drywall or fire-rated overspray.
    *   If a ceiling finish is not to be removed, nail rigid board insulation and drywall to it, ensuring all potential leakage paths are sealed, especially at ceiling-wall junctions.
    *   _Figure 7-11: Air seal and insulate between an unheated garage and the house (Illustration of a two storey home with an unfinished garage below a room of the house. The rear wall is a shared wall with the home so there is insulation between the framing studs. The ceiling of the garage forms the floor of the room above. The garage ceiling has a layer of rigid insulation sealed between the joists and covered by a layer batt insulation. Drywall covers this ceiling.)_

## 7.5 Additions and new construction

New construction offers an opportunity to install continuous air and vapour barriers and high levels of insulation efficiently and cost-effectively.

*   _Figure 7-12: Exterior wall cross-section (Cutaway of an exterior wall of a typical new one-storey house with insulated attic, cathedral ceiling and a full-depth concrete foundation. There is a window in the wall on the main floor. All walls, including the attic sloped ceiling, are insulated with a combination of batt and rigid insulation, have continuous vapour and/or moisture barriers and caulking along edges and joints.)_
*   **Renewable Energy**: Renovations allow for current or future integration of solar water heating, passive solar heating, radiant in-floor heating, photovoltaic power generation, and electric vehicle charging stations.
*   **Planning**: Ensure roof structure supports additional weight/wind loads for roof-mounted systems. Plan for future conduits, water piping, or electrical circuits.

**Key Features in New Construction (as per Figure 7-12):**

*   **Attic**: High levels of insulation, continuous air and vapour barrier, and ventilation. Roof trusses (raised heel, scissor, parallel chord) allow high insulation levels above outer walls.
*   **Walls**: Example shows a **2 x 6 wall with insulating sheathing**. Other systems include interior cross strapping, double-wall systems, and trusses, allowing the continuous air and vapour barrier part way in the wall. Note recessed headers for continuous air and vapour barrier and extra insulation.
*   **Windows**: High-performance **ENERGY STAR windows** used where possible, sealed to the air barrier.
*   **Foundation**: Full-depth insulation (exterior in example). Proper damp proofing, drainage system, and sloped grade ensure a dry basement.
*   **Mechanical systems**: Smaller space heating and cooling systems for well-insulated/air-sealed homes. High-efficiency space/water heating equipment can use directly connected combustion air.
