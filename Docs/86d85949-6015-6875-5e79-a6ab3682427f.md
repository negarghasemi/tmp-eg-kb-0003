# Reaching Net-Zero in Existing Buildings

### Overview

Buildings contribute approximately **18% of Canadas greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions**. Given that most buildings in use today will still be operational in 2050, Canada must retrofit nearly every existing building to meet its climate commitments.

Targeting the commercial building sector, which comprises about half a million structures, offers an effective and cost-efficient strategy for reducing emissions, as these buildings emit nearly as much as millions of residential ones. A practical regulatory framework is crucial to encourage faster and deeper energy and emissions reductions in existing buildings, ensuring they meet future needs and climate goals.

### Regulating Emissions in Existing Buildings

Canada is developing its first retrofit code, the **Alterations to Existing Buildings (AEB) code**, expected in 2024 and adopted by provinces and territories by late 2026. While a significant step, its requirements are based on upgrades initiated by building owners, meaning it may not sufficiently increase retrofit demand.

**Mandatory Building Performance Standards (MBPS)** are a vital complementary tool for Canadian policymakers. MBPS can significantly increase demand for retrofit activity, particularly in the **lowest-performing commercial buildings**, by setting performance limits that must be met by a specified date or a triggering event (e.g., major renovations, building transactions).

### The Power of Mandatory Building Performance Standards (MBPS)

MBPS are **legally binding** and carry the risk of liability and financial penalties for non-compliance. Well-designed MBPS programs also focus on equity, ensuring benefits are broadly distributed. While voluntary measures (benchmarking, labeling, financing, incentives) have raised awareness, they have largely been insufficient to drive the scale of retrofit activity needed to meet climate commitments, unlike MBPS.

### Canadian Mandatory Building Performance Standards (Timeline)

Canadian municipalities are emerging as leaders in reducing emissions in existing buildings. The following timeline highlights key developments, drawing on experiences from U.S.-based MBPS programs due to similarities in jurisdictional authority, climate, and building types and codes.

-   **2021: City of Montreal**
    -   Implemented mandatory carbon reporting and disclosure for large commercial and multi-unit residential buildings.
    -   Signals plans to implement MBPS in upcoming years, aligning with a future provincial benchmarking program.
-   **2022: City of Vancouver**
    -   Became the first Canadian municipality to adopt an MBPS program.
    -   Mandatory energy and carbon reporting, and carbon pollution limits for commercial and retail buildings, starting in 2026.
-   **2023: Metro Vancouver**
    -   Indicated its intention to regulate the performance of large commercial and multi-unit residential buildings.
    -   Includes adopting mandatory carbon reporting and disclosure.
-   **2024: City of Toronto**
    -   Will require mandatory emissions, energy, and water reporting.
    -   Mandatory building performance standards for large commercial buildings to low-rise residential homes.
