## Purpose of this Reference Guide

This guide helps address common compressed air system design and operating problems, providing guidance for optimizing performance. It is particularly useful if:

- Utility bills are rising, and energy costs need to be reduced.
- Replacing an old compressor or expanding the system is being considered.
- There's a need to improve the compressed air system but uncertainty on where to start.
- Production suffers from excessive downtime or insufficient air pressure.

**Why Compressed Air?**

Compressed air is **expensive to produce** and often consumes a significant portion of energy budgets. It is a controllable cost, and this guide aims to reduce associated energy, maintenance, and capital costs. Your air compressor system may be out of sight and out of mind, but its operating and maintenance costs can be substantial.

## What is Compressed Air?

Compressed air is a form of stored energy used to operate machinery, equipment, or processes, especially where electrical energy is impractical or hazardous. Typically, an air compressor converts approximately **7 volumes of atmospheric air into 1 volume of compressed air** (at about 100 psig), requiring significant energy. This process releases heat and moisture.

*Recovered heat from the air compressor can potentially be used as an energy efficiency measure for other processes, such as space and water heating.*

### Compressed Air Costs

Compressed air systems have significant lifetime ownership costs. Over the first ten years of a typical air-cooled compressor operating two shifts:

- **88%** of the total lifetime cost is for **operating costs** (electricity and maintenance).
- **12%** is for **original equipment and installation**.
- **76%** of the overall lifetime operating cost is for **electricity**.

**Recommendation:** Purchase decisions should be based on **overall expected lifetime operating costs**, not just initial equipment cost.

**Typical Lifetime Ownership Cost of Compressed Air Systems:**

| Cost Category              | Percentage |
| :------------------------- | :--------- |
| Electricity                | 76%        |
| Maintenance                | 12%        |
| Equipment and Installation | 12%        |

Roughly **90% of the energy** used to produce and distribute compressed air is typically **lost**. For every 100 HP energy input, only about 9 HP is useful work, with 80 HP lost as heat and compressor losses, 3 HP to dryers, 6 HP to filters and leaks, and 2 HP to pneumatic conversion.

*Always question if compressed air is the most appropriate power source for an end-use application. In many cases, a direct-drive electric tool is more efficient.*

## Introduction to Compressed Air Systems

Compressed air systems comprise **Supply** (compressors, air treatment, primary storage) and **Demand** (distribution piping, secondary storage, end-use equipment) sides.

*As a rule of thumb, for every **horsepower (HP)** in the nameplate capacity, the air compressor will produce approximately **4 standard cubic feet per minute (scfm)**.*

**Compressed Air Use**

Compressed air systems are often the **least energy-efficient** equipment in many facilities, presenting tremendous potential for energy efficiency improvements. Optimal performance requires managing both supply and demand sides.

*Properly managing a compressed air system can not only save electricity, but also decrease downtime, increase productivity, reduce maintenance, and improve product quality.*

For a typical compressed air end use, it takes about **10 units of electrical energy input** to the compressor to produce about **one unit of actual mechanical output**.

## Air Compressor Types and Controls

Two basic types of air compressors are positive displacement (rotary screw, reciprocating, vane) and dynamic.

**Compressor Motors**

*For new or replacement air compressors, **premium high-efficiency motors** should be specified over standard ones. The incremental cost is usually recovered quickly from energy savings.*

**Compressor Controls and System Performance**

Efficiently controlling flow at part loads is essential for energy savings. Control selection significantly impacts system performance and energy efficiency.

-   **Start/Stop:** Simplest, most efficient for smaller compressors (<30 HP). Motor turns on/off based on discharge pressure.
    *Caution: Repeated starts can overheat motors. Size storage receivers and maintain wide working pressure bands.*
-   **Load/Unload:** Keeps motor running, unloads compressor when pressure is adequate. Unloaded rotary screw compressors consume **15-35% of full load power**.
    *Load/unload control strategies require significant control storage receiver capacity for efficient part load operation.*

## Compressed Air Auxiliary Equipment

This equipment conditions compressed air to required specifications.

### Air Compressor Coolers

*Approximately **80 percent of the electrical energy** going to a compressor is converted into heat. This heat can be recovered for hot water or hot air, offering an excellent energy efficiency opportunity.*

-   **Compressor Intake:** Air entering the compressor should be as cool as possible for maximum energy efficiency (colder air is denser).
    *By ingesting an outdoor air intake supply (as opposed to air from a very warm compressor room), the energy efficiency is improved.*

### Air Dryers

Air dryers are necessary to remove moisture from compressed air, preventing corrosion and contamination.

*The typical pressure drop across a compressed air dryer is **3 to 5 psi**. Some undersized dryers cause even higher pressure drops. For ongoing energy efficiency, compressed air should be dried only to the dew point required, and with the appropriately sized dryer.*

### Filters

-   **Compressor Air Inlet Filter:** Should be replaced periodically. High differential reduces output capacity and decreases efficiency.
-   **Compressed Air Filters:** Downstream filters remove contaminants. Finer filters generally have higher pressure differentials.

*About **1% in higher energy costs** results from every **2 psi in filter differential**. If a given filter capacity is doubled the pressure loss across it will reduce by a factor of 4, for a 75% savings.*

*To save energy, minimize the filter pressure drop by using low differential mist eliminator style filters, oversized filters, or by using filters installed in parallel.*

**Example 1: Cost of Clogged Filters**

A 100 HP compressor operating for 2 shifts (annual electricity cost $32,330) with a 10 psi filter differential (requiring operation at 110 psi instead of 100 psi) incurs an energy penalty of approximately **5% or $1,615 per year**.

### Receivers and Air Storage

Adequate storage receiver capacity helps maintain air quality, system stability, and efficiency.

-   **Primary Air Receivers:** General system storage, damping pulsations, settling water/lubricant, supplying peak demands, reducing cycle frequencies.
    *As a rule of thumb, for load/unload operated lubricated screw compressors, the receiver volume should be **5 to 10 US gallons (20-40 liters) per trim compressor scfm output**.*
-   **Secondary Air Receivers:** Protection of sensitive end uses, managing transient users, stabilizing pressure in undersized piping.

**Example 2: Determining Size of Air Receiver**

For a clamp using 2 cfm, needing 85 psi for 2 minutes in a 100 psi system:

-   Cubic feet required = **2 cfm x 2 minutes = 4 cubic feet**
-   Pressure (psi) range = **100 - 85 = 15 psi**
-   Storage receiver required = **4 x 110 / 15 = 29 gallons (US)**

*Facilities having large fluctuations in air demand, or insufficient air pressure, should evaluate the need for one or more air receivers strategically located.*

### Separators and Drains

Drains remove condensate. Failed drains can overload dryers and foul equipment. Poorly designed or maintained drains waste compressed air.

*Replacing a manual drain constantly consuming **5 cfm of air** in a two-shift (4,250 hours per year) operation would save about **$425 per year**.*

### Piping

Undersized piping causes significant pressure loss, increasing compressor energy requirements.

*For every **2 psi pressure drop** caused by undersized or bottlenecked piping, there is approximately **1% increased energy required**.*

*To minimize energy loss from pressure differential and to help stabilize the end of line air pressures, the distribution system should be sized for no more than **2-3 psi pressure differential**.*

*If a pipe area is doubled, for a given flow, there is generally **one quarter the pressure drop** for the single diameter pipe - a **75% energy savings**.*

### Flow Controllers

Stabilize system pressure more precisely than compressor controls.

*Savings of about **1% per psi reduction** can be gained due to lower pressure if a significant portion of the load is unregulated.*

### Fittings

*Quick coupling fittings can be a major point of pressure drop, often causing up to **7 psi differential** per coupling. It is not uncommon to see pressure differentials of up to **30 psi** across hoses and couplings.*

## Uses and Misuses of Compressed Air

Compressed air is widely used, but its cost is often unknown, leading to inefficient use.

*Typically, less than **10% of the original energy** used to produce compressed air is actually converted into useful work. Consider converting end uses to other power sources.*

**Common Uses for Compressed Air:**

- Agitating liquids
- Air brakes
- Air piston powering
- Blending
- Bottling
- Clamping
- Cleaning
- Controls and actuators

- Cooling
- Dehydration

- Forming

- Mold press powering
- Packing
- Pressure treatment
- Sawing
- Seeding
- Snow making

- Sprinkler systems

## So You Want to Perform a Compressed Air System Assessment?

Evaluating your compressed air system is the first step to improving its energy efficiency. This involves a systems approach, examining individual components and their interactions.

*A qualified practitioner can assist you. Many utilities offer technical support and financial aid.*

**Common steps in an improvement program:**

- Gather equipment data (nameplate, volumes, piping, layout).
- Establish a baseline by measuring current performance levels.
- Establish required performance levels (pressure, power, air quality).
- Analyze data to identify improvement areas.
- Assess alternative configurations and measures.
- Determine best technical and economic options.
- Devise an implementation plan.
- Examine maintenance and purchasing practices.
- Factor in current deficiencies and future needs.
- Question if there's a better way to supply and use compressed air.

## Proven Energy Efficiency Measures

### Compressed Air System Leaks

Air leaks are a significant source of wasted energy, often accounting for **20-30% of a compressor's output**. Proactive leak management can reduce this to **less than 10%**.

*Fixing air leaks is most often the top priority, with a typical simple payback of **less than 6 months**.*

*Indifference to air leak management can lead to purchasing unnecessary air compressor capacity, increasing capital expenses.*

*The type of compressor control significantly affects leak reduction savings. For example, a 10% leak reduction with a modulating compressor might yield only 3% energy savings, while with a VSD compressor, it could be 10% energy savings.*

**Annual Cost of Compressed Air Leaks Based on $0.10/kWh Electricity Cost**

| Leak size    | 1 Shift (2250 hrs) | 2 Shifts (4250 hrs) | 3 Shifts (8400 hrs) |
| :----------- | :----------------- | :------------------ | :------------------ |
| **1/16\" leak** | **$200**           | **$380**            | **$750**            |
| **1/4\" leak**  | **$3,210**         | **$6,070**          | **$11,990**         |
| **3/8\" leak**  | **$7,230**         | **$13,650**         | **$26,980**         |
| **1/2\" leak**  | **$12,820**        | **$24,210**         | **$47,850**         |

**Estimating Total Air Leaks:**

-   Perform a low-load test during non-production time.
-   For load/unload compressors: Measure loaded and unloaded times. Leak load = (loaded time / total time) x compressor cfm output.
-   For modulating compressors: Use an accurate pressure gauge and stopwatch to measure pressure drop/rise times.
-   Approximate cost to feed leaks at 100 psi: **0.2 x leak cfm x hours per year x cost per kWh**.
    *A 100 cfm leak rate would cost about **$8,500 per year** to maintain (at 10 cents per kWh, 4,250 hours).*

**How to Track Down Air Leaks:**

-   Listen for leaks when the plant is shut down.
-   Use an ultrasonic leak detector in noisy environments.
-   Apply soapy water to suspected areas.

*The best time to find air leaks is when the plant is not operating, usually at night or on weekends.*

**Preventing Air Leaks:**

-   Install fittings properly with sealants.
-   Isolate non-operating equipment.
-   Lower system air pressure (reduces flow rate of leaks slightly).
-   Select high-quality fittings, hoses, and disconnects.

*Remember that once leaks have been repaired, the compressor control system often needs to be adjusted to achieve true energy savings.*

### Lower Compressor Discharge Pressure by Minimizing Pressure Drops

Compressor discharge pressure directly affects efficiency.

*For every **2 psi increase in discharge pressure**, energy consumption will increase by approximately **1 percent** at full output flow.*

*A properly designed compressed air system should have a pressure loss (or drop) of much less than **10 percent** of the compressor's discharge pressure.*

**Ways to Minimize Pressure Drops:**

-   Select equipment (aftercoolers, dryers, filters) with the lowest practical pressure drop.
-   Maintain air filtering and drying equipment.
-   Design distribution system with appropriate pipe diameter and looped configurations.
-   Reduce air travel distance.
-   Assess end-use pressure requirements and minimize system pressure accordingly.
-   Check air pressure at tool inlets; address pressure drops caused by undersized lines, quick couplers, FRLs, and hoses.

*End-use tool pressure differential can be easily diagnosed by using a test gauge setup with quick couplers.*

### Minimizing Compressed Air End Use Energy Requirements

-   Replace inappropriate end-use applications (e.g., open blowing) with efficient alternatives (e.g., vortex nozzles).
-   Install a flow controller to lower plant pressure and reduce artificial demand.
-   Turn off air-consuming equipment when not in use.
-   Avoid operating air tools without a load.
-   Replace worn tools.
-   Lubricate air tools as recommended.
-   Group end-use equipment with similar air requirements.

### Air Compressor Heat Recovery

*Approximately **80 percent of the electrical energy** used by an industrial air compressor is converted into heat. **50-90 percent** of this thermal energy can be recovered to offset space and water heating.*

-   **Heating Air:** Packaged rotary screw compressors are ideal for space heating. Approximately **50,000 BTU per hour** of heat can be extracted for each 100 scfm air for a compressor operating at full load.
-   **Heating Water:** Waste heat can be extracted from lubricant coolers to produce hot water. Typically, **50-60 percent** of compressor heat can be recovered for water heating.

### Implement More Efficient Compressor Control

-   Use advanced controls for multiple compressors to coordinate operation.
-   Consider time delays for compressor startup and ensure adequate storage receiver capacity.
-   Adjust cascaded pressure bands as needed.
-   The \"trim compressor\" should be capable of running efficiently at partial loads.

### Install Storage Capacity

-   Locate receivers as close to compressors as practical.
-   For load/unload rotary screw compressors, install **10 US gallons per cfm** of compressor capacity.
-   Consider oversizing receivers to stabilize system pressure and respond to intermittent demands.

### Optimize Air Dryers

-   For new purchases, choose **energy-saving cycling style refrigerated air dryers**.
-   Avoid drying air to a dew point lower than required.
-   Use **energy-saving dew point controllers** for all regenerative desiccant dryers.

### Reduce System Drainage

-   Use **airless drains** instead of timer drains or manual drains.
-   Regularly test automatic drain traps.

### Purchase a More Efficient Compressor

-   Base purchasing decisions on **overall expected life cycle operating cost**.
-   Purchase the most energy-efficient compressors, including those with **premium efficiency motors**.
-   Operate base load units at maximum capacity.
-   Consider a **Variable Speed Drive (VSD) compressor** for variations in flow above the base load.

## Maintaining Your Compressed Air System

Regular maintenance is crucial for energy efficiency. More frequent maintenance than recommended intervals is often wise, especially for leaks, pressures, moisture, and controls.

**Preventative Maintenance Checklist Considerations**

| Frequency or Running Hours | Action                                                                                                                                                                             |
| :------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Daily**                  | Check control panel and gauges; Record pressures, cooling water temperature; Check for abnormalities.                                                                              |
| **Weekly**                 | Inspect for Air Leaks; Inspect and replace filters; Check and adjust air regulators; Check and adjust system pressures; Check and adjust refrigerated dryer set points.                 |
| **Every 3,000 hours**      | Check and replace filter element; Check/change sump-breather filter element; Check/clean condensate drain valves; Inspect shaft couplings and fasteners; Lubricate motor bearings. |
| **Every 15,000 hours**     | Test all safety devices; Inspect and clean heat exchangers; Check and clean blowdown valves, check valves, interstage pipe works, isolation mounts; Inspect and clean lubricant sump. |

## Before and After Case Examples

### Case 1: Install Purge Controller, Repair Leaks and Lower Pressure

*Initial Situation:* Medium-sized furniture manufacturer with two 100 HP modulating screw compressors, 1,000 cfm desiccant dryer (uncontrolled purge), running 6,000 hours/year. Problems with end-use pressure led to increased compressor discharge pressure. Attempting to turn off one compressor caused low pressure during peak production.

**Before Audit Data:**

-   Total compressed air production: **450 cfm**
-   Total compressor energy consumption: **880,000 kWh**
-   System specific power: **32.9 kW/100 cfm**
-   Total electrical cost: **$88,000/year** (at $0.10/kWh)
-   Air dryer purge: **150 cfm**
-   Leaks: **100 cfm**
-   Drains: **5 cfm**
-   Average compressor discharge pressure: **120 psi**
-   Dryer and filter pressure differential: **10 psi**
-   End use piping differential: **20 psi**
-   Required end use pressure: **80 psi**

**After Implementation (Initial Recommendations):**

-   Leak reduction: **50 cfm**
-   Drainage reduction: **5 cfm**
-   Plant pressure reduction by 15 psi for 10% flow reduction: **25 cfm**
-   Install purge control on dryer with purge reduction: **117 cfm**
-   Total flow reduction: **197 cfm**
-   Resulted in final average compressor loading: **253 cfm**
-   One compressor could be turned off.
-   Remaining unit consumption: **76 kW x 6000 hrs = 456,000 kWh**
-   **Savings with existing modulating control: $42,400 per year (48%)**

**After Further Measures (VSD Compressor and Optimization):**

-   Reduced end use piping differential by 13 psi.
-   Reduced dryer and filter differential by 7 psi.
-   Compressor discharge pressure reduction: 20 psi.
-   Installed VSD compressor (average power at 253 cfm@100 psi: 50 kW).
-   New system electrical consumption: **300,000 kWh**
-   New system specific power: **19.8 kW/100 cfm**
-   New system electrical cost: **$30,000 per year**
-   **Total Savings: 66%**

### Case 2: Use Smaller Compressor During Off-Hours

*Situation:* The furniture manufacturer needed 5 cfm of compressed air continuously for a dry fire system, including weekends (2,760 extra hours/year), with no production.

### Case 3: On/Off vs. Load/No Load Control

*Situation:* An automobile dealership considering upgrading from a 15 HP reciprocating compressor (50 cfm @ 175 psi, 13 kW average) to a new 15 HP screw compressor (58 cfm @ 150 psi, 13.5 kW). Average flow is 10 cfm with 50 cfm peak. System operates 3,745 hours/year at 145 psi. Dryer consumes 0.88 kW, filters and dryer cause 8 psi pressure drop, timer drain consumes 2 cfm.

**Existing System Cost:**

-   Compressor consumption: **9,737 kWh/year = $974/year**
-   Dryer consumption: **3,296 kWh/year = $330/year**
-   **Total system cost: $1,304/year**

**Proposed New Screw Compressor (Load/Unload with Standard Dryer):**

-   New compressor running in load/unload mode, rapid cycling due to pressure drops.
-   Compressor will consume ~60% of full load power.
-   Proposed compressor consumption: **13.5 kW x 0.6 x 3,745 hrs = 30,335 kWh/year = $3,034/year**
-   Dryer consumption: **$330/year**
-   **Total system costs: $3,364/year**

*The new screw compressor would cost about **2.5 times as much** in electricity to operate.*

**Alternative High-Efficiency System (On/Off Operation with Measures Applied):**

-   Reduced pressure differential (filters 0.5 psi, new cycling dryer 3 psi).
-   Average compressor discharge pressure reduced to 115 psi.
-   Compressor selected for immediate shut-off (on/off control).
-   Drain reduced to zero with airless drain.
-   Added storage receiver to reduce compressor starts.
-   Added pressure regulating valve to reduce shop pressure to 100 psi.

**Energy Efficiency Measures Outcome:**

-   Air flow reduction: 2 cfm (drain) + 2 cfm (pressure reduction).
-   Compressor power consumption reduced by 15% due to 30 psi reduction.
-   Proposed compressor consumption: **4,455 kWh/year = $446/year**
-   Dryer consumption: **140 kWh/year = $14/year**
-   **Total system costs: $460/year**

*The efficient system consumes about **35% of the original system's energy** and **14% of the proposed load/unload system's energy**.*

## Next Steps

Compressed air optimization is an ongoing process. Start with \"low-hanging fruit\":

-   Fix air leaks.
-   Lower system pressures.
-   Optimize control settings.

Once results are seen, communicate them to management and employees to gain support for further investments (e.g., more efficient compressors, updated distribution systems, ongoing leak management). Always factor in the **lifetime operating cost** when designing or purchasing components. Work with your purchasing department to procure the most energy-efficient options.

## Quick Tips to Optimize Compressed Air Systems

1.  Identify and repair air leaks, starting with the biggest.
2.  Measure and baseline your compressed air system to determine operating costs and efficiency.
3.  Operate compressed air system at the lowest practical pressure.
4.  Install, adjust, and maintain automatic system controls to coordinate operations of air compressors.
5.  Turn off compressed air supply to zones, equipment, and applications that are not operating.
6.  Use a blower rather than a compressor where appropriate.
7.  Use air receivers to reduce compressor cycling and respond to peak air demands.
8.  Use larger diameter pipes and looped air distribution configurations where practical.
9.  Maintain compressed air equipment, filters, and drains at an appropriate level.
10. Constantly ask if compressed air is the best utility for the intended application.

*Remember to involve compressed air operating and maintenance personnel in production and end-use decisions.*
