# Canadian Heat Pump Myth Buster

_Authored by: Sarah Riddell (Policy Research Associate, Clean Heat) & Aidan Belanger (Communications Specialist)_

_Date: August 1, 2023_

## Introduction to Heat Pumps

Heat pumps are a crucial technology for electrifying buildings and achieving net-zero emissions targets by 2050. They efficiently use electricity for both heating and cooling by transferring heat, effectively replacing fossil fuels.

**Key Benefits:**
- Increased comfort
- Often lower utility costs
- Efficient cooling (increasingly essential due to climate change)

**Impact & Efficiency:**
- Can reduce global greenhouse gas emissions by more than **500 million tonnes in 2030**.
- Operate **three times more efficiently** than electric resistance or electric boiler heating.
- Over **840,000 heat pumps** have been installed in Canadian homes to date.

## Types of Heat Pumps

- **Air-Source Heat Pumps (ASHPs):** Reduce electricity consumption for space heating by at least 50% compared to electric resistance heating.
- **Ground-Source Heat Pumps (GSHPs):** Operate more efficiently at lower temperatures than ASHPs due to warmer and more stable underground temperatures.
- **Air-to-Water Heat Pumps:** A subcategory of ASHPs that absorb heat from outside air and transfer it to a buildings hydronic circuit.
- **Supplementary Systems:** Required in climates where temperatures drop below the installed heat pump's rated capacity (e.g., electric resistance built into the heat pump, electric baseboards, or an existing gas system).

## Heat Pump Myth Busters

_Share the myths on social media by using the hashtag #HeatPumpMyths_

### Myth: Heat pumps are a new and untested technology.

**Fact:** Heat pumps have a long history of successful use, with the underlying technology dating back to the 18th century.
- They transfer heat using the same technology as refrigerators and air conditioners, with recent advancements enabling them to work in colder climates.
- Artificial refrigeration was first demonstrated in 1850, and the first documented heat pump followed shortly after in 1856. Heat pumps have been used in Canada since at least 1948.
- Modern heat pumps are distinguished by their ability to maintain capacity and heat during cold outdoor temperatures.

### Myth: Canada would be alone if requirements for cleaner and more efficient heating were introduced.

**Fact:** Many countries in Europe are eliminating fossil fuel-based heating systems, and several US jurisdictions are regulating them.
- Austria, France, Ireland, the Netherlands, and Norway all have bans on oil and gas boiler installation in new buildings by 2023.
- New York State prohibits natural gas and other fossil fuel heating and cooking equipment in new buildings shorter than seven stories by 2026, and by 2029 for taller buildings.
- San Francisco will require all new water heaters and furnaces to have zero emissions of nitrogen oxides, effectively banning fossil fuels in buildings (excluding cooking appliances).

### Myth: Canada is too cold for heat pumps.

**Fact:** Heat pumps are a proven technology in cold climates.
- Cold Climate Air Source Heat Pump (CC-ASHP) technology is specifically designed for northern climates, with the newest prototypes heating down to **31 C**.
- Heat pumps alone can heat more than **90%** of the time in Canadas coldest cities.
- Heat pump technology is continuously improving substantially.

### Myth: A heat pump is more expensive than a fossil-fuel-based heating system.

**Fact:** A heat pump is less expensive over the lifespan of the equipment in almost every scenario due to higher efficiency. Federal, provincial, and municipal incentive programs help lower upfront costs.
- Homeowners switching from an oil furnace to a CC-ASHP could expect to save **$1,000-$3,500** in annual utility costs.
- Switching from electric resistance heating to a CC-ASHP generated **$700-$1,900** in savings, depending on electricity rates and the climate.
- Savings from switching from natural gas ranged from **$50-$150 per year** in most regions of Canada, including the fixed natural gas charges (**$150-$300/year**) eliminated by going fully electric.

### Myth: Electric grids dont have the capacity for heat pumps.

**Fact:** Electricity grid operators can manage the transition to electric heat through various strategies, including more energy efficiency, more innovative timing of electricity demands, and backup fuels.
- Electrification will occur gradually, allowing electricity system planners to plan ahead and respond.
- Some regions of Canada already use a lot of electric heat, and heat pumps will save electricity by replacing less efficient electric heating systems.
- Peak demand during extreme cold periods can be managed with better building envelopes, smart demand-side management, and hybrid heating.

### Myth: In provinces with dirty grids, heat pumps dont lower emissions.

**Fact:** Heat pumps can lower emissions even on grids with higher carbon intensity because they are so energy efficient.
- All Canadian provinces have sufficiently clean electricity grids for heat pumps to lower emissions.
- Coupling heat pumps with other renewable energies or strategic use of fuels can shift away from peak demand.
- With the federal government targeting net-zero emissions electricity by 2035, the emissions savings achieved by heat pumps will continue to improve as the grid becomes increasingly cleaner.

### Myth: Heat pumps are not climate friendly because of their refrigerants.

**Fact:** While most heat pumps in Canada currently contain higher global warming potential (GWP) refrigerants, emissions savings are still positive, and low GWP refrigerants are gaining traction.
- Even with todays refrigerants, emissions are reduced by **20-80%** compared to fossil fuel heating systems.
- Building codes need to be updated to allow heat pumps with lower GWP refrigerants (already available in Europe and the United States) into more Canadian buildings.
- Good maintenance and installation practices reduce refrigerant leaks.
- Installing the correct system size can also reduce the likelihood of refrigerant leaks.

### Myth: A gas furnace can still heat if there is an electrical power outage.

**Fact:** Natural gas furnaces need electricity to operate.
- Improved building envelopes are a better way to promote resilience during power outages, as indoor temperatures would drop more slowly.
- Smart home electrification systems can introduce the ability to use electric vehicle batteries during emergencies.
- Heat pumps can increase resilience to extreme heat through air conditioning.

### Myth: Heat pumps will not work in old homes without extensive upgrades.

**Fact:** Heat pumps work in almost all buildings. While they perform better when heat can stay inside for longer, they can be chosen over stand-alone fossil fuel systems even with minimal existing insulation and air sealing, with future envelope upgrades considered when sizing the heat pump.
- Heat pumps are an excellent choice in home retrofit projects, as there are many different types that work with various existing HVAC designs.
- Policies and programs should aim to coordinate building envelope upgrades with heat pumps as much as possible.
- Promoting longer-term energy plans, instead of emergency replacements, is the best way to optimize building envelope and heat system upgrades.

### Myth: HVAC contractors do not support heat pumps.

**Fact:** Contractor associations support heat pumps, seeing them as opportunities for better work and customer experiences.
- Associations representing HVAC contractors have written in support of the transition to net-zero emissions.
- Heat pumps present new business opportunities for HVAC contractors and can attract individuals seeking environmentally friendly jobs within the trades.
- Lists of certified heat pump installers are available from heat pump manufacturers websites.
