# Ground-Source Heat Pumps

## Overview

**Major Drawback:** The high initial cost of ground-source heat pump systems, primarily due to the underground heat exchanger.

**Opportunities for Improvement:**

*   **Cost Reduction:** Achieve savings through a better understanding of heat exchanger behavior and its interaction with the ground, leading to the development of smaller, more efficient, and less expensive exchangers.
*   **Refrigerant Efficiency:** More detailed information on the behavior of new refrigerants is needed to maximize component efficiency.
*   **Environmental Impact:** Development of new refrigerants that are less harmful to the environment.
*   **Community Applications:** Lack of existing standards for community-based heating applications leads to contractor reluctance. Demonstration sites are crucial for data collection, guideline development, and validating feasibility for community applications.

### Development of a ground-source heat pump with CO2 in a secondary loop and a phase change

Carbon dioxide (CO2) is considered the best alternative to synthetic refrigerants due to its thermophysical and transport properties, and environmental benefits. A research project is underway to:

*   Study the behavior of a ground-source heat pump system using CO2 with a phase change in a secondary ground loop.
*   Develop a theoretical model to predict thermal and hydrodynamic phenomena within the ground heat exchanger.
*   Utilize a test bed to validate the theoretical model and study the dynamic behavior of exchangers.

### Direct expansion (DX) systems

DX systems are a preferred option for ground-source heat pumps in North America. However, there's a need for technical data to optimize these systems and replace synthetic refrigerants to address environmental concerns. The project aims to:

*   Study the replacement of R-22 with more environmentally friendly refrigerants like R-410a.
*   Conduct field tests.
*   Develop a simulation model.

### Ground-source storage for community heating

This research involves:

*   Follow-up and assessment of existing community heating systems that integrate ground-source heat pumps into thermal energy storage systems.
*   Analysis of systems equipped with controllers for efficient heating and air conditioning management between residential and exothermic buildings (e.g., supermarkets, arenas).
*   Using assessment results to develop guidelines and improve the planning and development of such systems.

*Managed by CanmetENERGY at the Varennes (Quebec) research centre.*
