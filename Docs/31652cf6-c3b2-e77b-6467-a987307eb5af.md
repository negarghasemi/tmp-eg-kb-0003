# Keeping The Heat In - Section 4: Comprehensive air leakage control in your home

Air leakage control is the **single most important retrofit activity** and should be considered first in any upgrade strategy for your house. It is essential to ensure that moisture does not enter the insulation or building envelope when insulating or upgrading the air barrier system. Comprehensive air leakage control involves systematically identifying and sealing as many air leakage paths as possible using weatherstripping, caulking, gaskets, and tapes. Potential leak areas include windows, doors, wall cracks, interior trim, and even ceiling light fixtures.

## Contracting the work

While homeowners can perform effective air sealing with patience, **professional air sealers** often achieve superior results due to their experience. Professionals may use:

-   **Depressurizing fan door (blower door)** with smoke pencils to locate leaks.
-   **Specialty caulking and sealants**.
-   **Infrared scanners** (in conjunction with a blower door test) to find air leaks and missing insulation.

Many air sealing companies also offer **testing and assessment of ventilation and combustion air requirements**, including backdrafting tests for combustion gases.

## Effect on a house as a whole

As a house's envelope becomes tighter, **household humidity levels can rise**, potentially leading to condensation and moisture problems. Additionally, **less fresh air circulates**, reducing the air available for combustion appliances. Therefore, comprehensive air leakage control must include attention to controllable **whole-house ventilation** and **combustion-air supply**.

-   Proper ventilation ensures **healthier indoor air quality** and controls humidity.
-   Furnaces, water heaters, fireplaces, woodstoves, and other fuel-burning appliances require adequate air for combustion and exhausting gases. Insufficient air can lead to **dangerous backdrafting**.
-   Each house responds uniquely to air sealing; therefore, **monitoring is important**.
-   Older houses may need remedial measures (e.g., cleanup and replacement of mouldy materials, improved air and vapour barriers) before air sealing.

*Humidity, ventilation, and combustion air are discussed in more detail in Section 9, \"Operating your house.\"*

## 4.1 Finding leakage areas

Air leaks occur where there is a hole in the building envelope and a pressure difference. During winter, houses often exhibit a \"stack effect,\" where air enters at lower levels and exits at upper levels and the ceiling.

### 4.1.1 How to locate air leaks

For optimal results, hiring an air sealing contractor or professional energy advisor is recommended. However, you can also perform DIY leak detection:

-   **DIY Leak Detector**: Use burning incense sticks. Hold two or three together for more smoke. Powerful leaks will dissipate the smoke, while slower leaks will cause the smoke to trail or move towards the leak.
    *Figure 4-1 Do-it-yourself leak detector: Illustration of a hand holding two burning incense sticks next to an electrical outlet with smoke drifting away from the outlet.* 
-   **Checking for drafts**: On a cold and windy day, check suspected areas. This makes leaks easier to locate.
-   **Interior walls and features**: Check electrical outlets and switches, as they can have direct routes to the outside through partition walls or along floor joists.
-   **Rudimentary fan test**: Close all windows and doors. Turn on all exhaust appliances (bathroom/kitchen fans, clothes dryers on cool cycle, portable window fans). **Crucially, turn off all fuel-fired space and water heaters beforehand to prevent backdrafting of combustion gases.** Then, use your leak detector to identify and mark leaks.

### 4.1.2 Checklist of air leakage areas

**Inside the main living areas, check the following:**

-   **Windows**: Glazings for tightness, and around the window sash and casing.
-   **Doors**: Around the door, including the threshold and door frame.
-   **Electrical outlets and switches**: Including those on interior walls.
-   **Exhaust fans and vents**: Ensure they vent outside and close properly when not in use.
-   **Corners**: Where two walls meet with an imperfect seal.
-   **Light fixtures**: In the ceiling.
-   **Interior trim and baseboards**.
-   **Cracks**: In the wall finish or ceiling.
-   **Joints**: Where a wood frame wall meets a masonry wall or chimney.
-   **Doors and hatches**: Into unheated attics.
-   **Fireplace dampers and bricks**.
-   **Behind bathtubs and under sinks**.
-   **Above sliding pocket doors**.
-   **Around plumbing pipes and ductwork**.

**Inside the attic, check the following (you may need to move existing insulation):**

-   **Plumbing stack and other pipes**: Entering the attic.
-   **Wires or ceiling light fixtures**: That penetrate the attic floor.
-   **Ducting**: That enters the attic from inside the house.
-   **Ceiling junctions**: With interior wall partitions.
-   **Attic access doors**.
-   **Chimneys**.
-   **Shared walls**.
-   **Ceiling areas**: In bathrooms and above stairwells.

**Inside the basement, check the following:**

-   **Sill plate**: Where the wood-frame wall meets the masonry (concrete or stone) foundation, or where joists penetrate the masonry wall.
-   **Holes or gaps**: Where electrical service, gas service, or oil fill pipes go through the wall. *Be careful around electrical wires and gas pipes; do not disturb connections.*
-   **Holes**: For wiring, cabling, plumbing, and air conditioning pipes going into internal and external walls.
-   **Leaky ducting or poorly fitted hot air registers or cold air intakes**.
-   **Window and door framing**.
-   **Cracks**: In the foundation wall and slab.
-   **Floor drains**.
-   **Base of the chimney or flue**.

**Safety warning**: *Refer to Part 1.4, \"Health and safety considerations,\" for a warning about asbestos and vermiculite insulation.*

## 4.2 Caulking and other air sealing materials

**Air seal cracks and penetrations** on the inside surface of exterior walls, ceilings, or floors to prevent air from escaping into the walls and roof.

**On the exterior**, caulk cracks that could allow water and insect entry. If painting, avoid plugging siding joints and use permeable paint/stain to allow walls to dispel moisture. Special sealers are available for stucco.

### 4.2.1 Caulking basics

-   **Caulking gun**: Buy a good quality gun that fits your hand, with a pressure release lever. A thumb release, nozzle cutter, and tube piercer are convenient.
-   **Sealant choice**: Choose the right sealant for the job. Use premium caulks for durability.
-   **Practice**: Practice running and smoothing beads before applying them to the actual areas.
    *Figure 4-3 Sealing air and vapour barrier seams: Cutaway of a wall showing five steps to installing polyethylene and vapour barriers.* 
    *Figure 4-4 How to lay a bead of caulking: Illustration of a tube of caulking, with caulking being applied to a small gap where a vertical and a horizontal surface meet.*

**Guidelines for applying caulking (for air/vapour barrier seams):**

-   Use **wide sheets** to minimize seams.
-   **Overlap all seams and edges** over a solid backing (e.g., a stud).
-   Apply a bead of **non-hardening acoustical sealant** between overlapped sheets over the support.
-   **Staple through the sheets and sealant bead**. Minimize other staples.
-   The finish (e.g., drywall) anchors the seam. If polyethylene is recessed, a batten nailed over the seam provides mechanical support.
-   **Seal all penetrations**. Where possible, penetrate at a solid backing (plywood) or drill through single/double studs.

**Safety warning**: *Use **non-combustible caulking** around heat sources (chimney, light fixtures, fan motors, etc.). Silicone or polysulphide sealants work well; special high-temperature silicones are available for flue pipes. Always check product labels.*

### 4.2.2 Other sealing materials and applications

Other materials used for air barriers include:

-   **Specialty gaskets and tapes**.
-   **Sheet materials**: Polyethylene, spun-bonded olefin, rigid insulation, drywall, plywood, Oriented Strand Board (OSB), and sheet metal.

**Installation techniques are critical**: All edges, seams, and penetrations in sheet materials must be sealed.

### 4.2.3 Air and vapour barriers

-   A new air and vapour barrier can often be installed by using **sealed drywall as the air barrier** and layers of paint or sheet polyethylene as the vapour barrier.
-   Alternatively, **sealed sheet polyethylene** installed on the warm side of the insulation can act as both an air and vapour barrier. Proper installation is critical.

### 4.2.4 Refinishing the interior

-   If an attic retrofit is part of interior renovations, consider removing the ceiling and installing a new **sheet-polyethylene or sealed drywall air barrier** on the underside of the ceiling joists.
-   Maintaining continuity at interior partitions requires careful work:
    -   **Perpendicular to ceiling joists**: Maintain continuity by working from above, using connecting strips of polyethylene or extruded polystyrene.
    -   **Parallel to ceiling joists**: Install blocking and nailing strips for new ceiling material support.

### 4.2.5 Tips on sealing some of the leakiest areas

#### Electrical outlets

-   Seal any drafty electrical outlets, including interior ones. **Turn off power** at the circuit breaker/fuse before working.
-   For a better seal, caulk the gasket before installation. Special approved foam pads fit between the cover plate and receptacles.
-   Use child safety plugs with gaskets in seldom-used outlets.
-   During renovations, install outlets in special plastic boxes from building supply stores for optimal seals. Caulk wire penetrations and seal the new air and vapour barrier to the box edge.

#### Trim areas

-   Seal air leakage around all baseboards, mouldings, and window/door casings.
-   Use a flexible, clear, paintable, or matching-coloured caulk.
-   For more effectiveness, remove trim and seal behind it. Insulate wide cracks with a foam backer rod and seal with caulking or polyurethane foam.
-   If removing baseboards, caulk between the wall finish and bottom wall plates, and between plates and the floor.
    *Figure 4-5 Sealing behind window or door trim: Cutaway of a properly air sealed window or door trim.*

#### Glass panes (glazings)

-   Ensure a tight seal between glass and its wood frame. Check for cracks or missing sections in seals.
-   Repair with **glazing compound**, which lasts longer and stays semi-soft compared to putty. Remove old putty, apply new compound firmly with a putty knife, and then paint, allowing paint to just touch the glass.

#### Fireplaces

-   **Close the chimney damper** when not in use. Ensure it closes fully; if not, have it repaired by a certified chimney specialist.
-   Even with a closed damper, heat can escape. Install **tight-fitting glass doors** (ensure the door frame is tightly sealed to the fireplace with non-combustible caulk).
-   The frame should include a **combustion air gate or damper** to supply outside air to the fire. Close it when not in use.
-   Seal an unused or seasonally used fireplace opening with an **airtight plug** made of board material, cloth-covered, and providing a good edge seal.
-   Check for air leaks where the chimney meets the wall. Caulk this joint with **high-temperature caulking**.
    *Figure 4-6 Sealing an unused open fireplace: Cutaway of a fireplace indicating the damper at the base of the chimney, the combustion air supply and damper connected by a vent in the floor of the fireplace to the exterior, and an insulated board in front of the fireplace opening that can serve as a plug.*

#### Chimney

-   Chimneys present unique challenges for air-sealing and insulating.

#### Attic hatch

-   Seal the attic hatch like an exterior door. Caulk around the frame and between the casing and ceiling drywall.
-   Weatherstrip along the edges of the casing or access panel.
-   Install hooks with eyebolts or latches to hold the hatch firmly against the weatherstripping.
-   The hatch itself should be **insulated**.
    *Figure 4-7 Cutaway view of a well-sealed attic hatch: There is sealant between the casing and the ceiling drywall. A hook with an eyebolt ensures that the hatch is held firmly against the weatherstripping.*

**Safety warning**: *Fire safety is a real concern when air-sealing and insulating. Refer to Part 5.1.2, \"Fire and other hazards in attics,\" for methods of dealing with this area.*

#### Windows that are never opened

-   Seal windows not needed for ventilation or emergency escape with **caulking** instead of weatherstripping.
-   Use **strippable caulk** if the window needs to be opened again in the future.
