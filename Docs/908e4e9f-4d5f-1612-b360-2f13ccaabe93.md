# Net-Zero Buildings in Canada

## What are building codes?
Building codes are regulatory instruments that set requirements for the design and construction of residential or commercial buildings. They ensure new construction meets minimum health, safety, and performance standards. When model codes are adopted into law, they become the minimum requirement for health, safety, and well-being related to building construction and occupancy.

Building codes are enforced by the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) through the building permit process.

### Key Definitions:
*   **Model codes**: A set of suggested rules and practices that are only considered law when adopted.
*   **Adopted codes**: Model codes enacted by a provincial, territorial, or municipal government.
*   **Building energy code**: A regulatory standard that sets minimum efficiency requirements for new and renovated buildings, aiming to reduce energy use and emissions. Energy codes are a subset of building codes.

## The Urgency for Net-Zero Buildings

### Key Statistics and Impacts:
*   **Emissions Reduction**: Adoption of net-zero energy-ready standards is projected to produce **20% fewer emissions per year**. *Provinces and territories have agreed to reach net-zero energy-ready standard by 2030, but adoption of tiered codes has been limited.*
*   **Existing Building Stock**: **Most buildings standing today will continue to be in service in 2050**. Therefore, there is a critical need to retrofit nearly every existing building with a practical regulatory framework to encourage faster, deeper energy and emissions reductions.
*   **Retrofit Rate Target**: The national rate of building retrofits needs to rise to at least **2.5% each year by 2030**, up from less than 1% today. *Deep retrofits can target reductions in energy use by 70%, and emissions by 80% or greater.*

### GHG Emissions from Buildings:
*   Canadian residential and commercial buildings account for **13% of Canadas GHG emissions**. This rises to **18%** when considering electricity used for cooling, lighting, and appliance usage, and even higher when accounting for upstream impacts of manufacturing building materials or fossil fuel-intensive construction processes.
*   Despite significant reductions in energy use over past decades, energy demand and building-related emissions are expected to rise. This could account for half of the remaining 2C carbon budget over the next 40 years.
*   Decarbonizing Canadas building sector requires a transformation in how new and existing buildings are designed, constructed, and retrofitted, emphasizing a new role for building energy codes.
