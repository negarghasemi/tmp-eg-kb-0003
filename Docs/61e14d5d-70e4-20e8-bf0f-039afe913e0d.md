# Keeping The Heat In - Section 6: Basement insulation: floors, walls and crawl spaces

Basements can account for **about 25 percent of a homes total heat loss** due to large, uninsulated surface areas and air leakage. Many basements have little to no insulation, offering significant potential for improvement.

## Types of Basement Construction

-   **Full foundation basement**: Most common, with mainly below-grade foundation walls.
-   **Partial depth foundation walls**: Create a crawl space.
-   **Posts and piers**: Older homes, cottages, or mobile homes with open or walled-off space below.
-   **Slab-on-grade**: No basement or crawl space.

*Expert advice is recommended before proceeding with remediation for any type.* 

### Concrete Foundations

-   **Poured or concrete block**: Built since the 1920s, usually with parging, waterproofing, and drain tiles. Good candidates for inside or outside insulation if no serious water or structural problems exist.
-   **New houses or new foundations**: Allow about **one year for drying** before insulating or renovating due to high moisture content in concrete.
-   **Older rubble, brick or stone**: Often uneven, rarely damp-proofed, and high mortar content can absorb water. Best insulated from the outside, but some interior insulation is possible. **Always seek expert advice.**
-   **Preserved wood foundations**: Newer homes, generally damp-proofed and fully insulated.

## Basement Assessment

Before planning insulation work, assess the basement for the following issues:

### Water Leaks

-   **Major leaks (persistent, flooding)**: Must be corrected by excavating, damp-proofing/waterproofing, adding drainage, and insulating from the exterior.
-   **Minor leaks**: Can be corrected by sloping the grade away from the foundation, directing downspouts, and patching the interior foundation.
-   **Sump pumps/sewer backup**: Correct any problems before insulating.

### Dampness

-   **Symptoms**: Staining, mould growth, blistering/peeling paint, efflorescence (whitish mineral deposit), spalling (surface deterioration), musty smell.
-   **Correction**: Minor dampness from interior; serious problems from exterior.
-   **Condensation**: Can form on cool foundation walls in humid summers.

#### Checking for Dampness

-   **Purpose**: Limit mould, odours, water damage. Reveal moisture wicking through foundations.
-   **Method**: Tape a **1 m (40 in.) square plastic sheet** to concrete walls/slab (or hold down on earth floors). Check after **one to two days**.
    -   **Condensation on top**: Indicates high interior moisture levels.
    -   **Condensation on bottom or wet concrete/soil**: Indicates moisture movement from the ground, and potential radon emissions.
-   **Remediation**: Any renovation will require concrete sealing, waterproofing, or subfloor membranes to deal with dampness or add a moisture barrier.

### Cracks

-   **Active cracks (growing/shrinking)**: Seek professional help for structural repairs.

### Radon

-   May be present in all homes, regardless of humidity. (Refer to Part 1.4 for health and safety considerations).

## Insulate Inside or Outside?

-   **Insulating on the outside is generally best**, but inside insulation is often more economical and practical. Sometimes a combination is needed.

### Insulating Inside

-   **Methods**: Rigid insulation board and drywall, wood-frame wall and insulation, or other combinations.
-   **Advantages**:
    -   Can be incorporated into basement finishing plans.
    -   Can be done year-round, one part at a time, often as a DIY project.
    -   Easier and cheaper to achieve high insulation values for the full wall.
    -   No disturbance to landscaping or driveway.
-   **Disadvantages**:
    -   **Do not insulate basements with moisture problems** (leaks, dampness, efflorescence, blistering paint) from the inside without prior correction.
    -   Interior insulation makes foundation walls colder, increasing condensation risk.
    -   Interior finishes hide moisture problems, leading to long-term mould exposure.
    -   Obstructions (electrical panels, wiring, plumbing, stairs) make work difficult and reduce effectiveness.
-   *Technical Note*: Frost action and structural damage from inside insulation is generally not a problem, but check with local authorities in frost-susceptible soils or extreme climates.

### Insulating Outside

-   **Method**: Excavating around the foundation, waterproofing, installing insulation, flashing, and protective covering.
-   **Advantages**:
    -   Outside wall is often more continuous and easier to insulate.
    -   Allows for visual inspection and correction of moisture or structural problems (rubble/brick foundations, water leakage, dampness).
    -   No disruption inside the house; no interior space lost.
    -   Eliminates freeze-thaw stresses; frost unlikely to reach footings.
    -   Foundation mass within the insulated envelope helps stabilize temperatures.
-   **Disadvantages**:
    -   Excavation is difficult/risky (manual) or requires machinery access.
    -   Dirt storage can be an issue.
    -   Cannot be done in winter; high water table can be problematic.
    -   Obstacles (non-removable steps, paved carports, landscaping) complicate work.
    -   Rubble/brick foundations may require expert advice before excavation.
    -   High insulation levels can be expensive and may affect house appearance.

## 6.1 Insulating the Basement from the Outside

**Before starting**: Assess outside features (services, access, lot lines), insulation requirements (type, thickness, height, depth), finishing details (protective coating, flashing), and tools/equipment.

### 6.1.1 Special Safety Considerations

-   **Safety Warning**: Follow safe trenching practices to prevent accidental burial and collapse. Protect trenches from water and ensure people/animals cannot fall in. Brace unstable soils. Consult local building authorities for shoring/trenching advice.
-   **Underground services**: Locate all gas, electricity, telephone, water, and sewage lines before digging.

#### How to Insulate Outside the Basement

Plan for several weeks, especially if excavation, repairs, waterproofing, and drainage system installation are needed.

1.  **Dig the trench**:
    -   Ideally to footings (never below).
    -   Wide enough to work in; avoid oversized trenches.
    -   Store excavated dirt on a tarp **at least 600 mm (24 in.)** from excavation.
    -   *Minimum depth option*: Insulate to **600 mm (24 in.) below grade** if foundation/drainage are good, combined with interior insulation. An optional **horizontal rigid insulation skirt** can reduce heat loss and frost problems.
2.  **Prepare the surface and site**:
    -   Clean foundation (wire brush, scraper, pressure-washer).
    -   Inspect and repair major holes, cracks, damage; seal penetrations.
    -   Smooth/replace deteriorated surfaces and old parging; allow to dry.
    -   Check/repair drainage tiles. Install new drainage system if needed (ensure proper discharge). Consult a drainage expert.
    -   Apply **waterproofing material from grade level down to and over footings**; seal penetrations/overlaps. Sheet materials, sprays, roll-on compounds available. Follow manufacturer directions.
    -   **Dimple membrane**: Creates air gaps for drainage. Some types may require additional waterproofing. Check manufacturer for product suitability and installation.
3.  **Apply the insulation**:
    -   **Types**: Rigid mineral wool boards, high-density polystyrene (Type IV), polyurethane/polyisocyanurate boards.
    -   **Type IV polystyrene board**: Most commonly used for exterior below-grade applications.
    -   **Insulation with drainage capability (e.g., mineral wool board)**: Must be applied to **full depth**, with a drainage system, and no horizontal seams.
    -   **Installation**: Measure/cut to desired height (footings to flashing). Start at a corner, overlap, keep tight to wall. Use corrosion-resistant fasteners. Below-grade portion held by backfill.
    -   **Overlapping joints**: Some experts suggest two layers.
    -   **Interlocking systems**: Grooved polystyrene boards with steel channels (for above-grade to **300 mm (12 in.) depth**).
    -   **Rubble/brick foundations**: Apply parging first to smooth. Waterproof parging before insulating.
4.  **Apply the flashing**:
    -   Keeps insulation in place, prevents water intrusion, provides neat junction.
    -   **Considerations**: Location (how high up wall insulation extends) and type.
    -   **Z-flashing**: Use if siding can be removed/pried up, inserted **at least 50 mm (2 in.)** behind siding/building paper.
    -   **Metal J-channel or wood flashing**: If Z-flashing isn't possible (e.g., brick).
    -   **Sloping**: Wood flashing should be sloped with **at least 20 mm ( in.) overhang** and drip edge.
    -   **Sealing**: Caulk joint between flashing and house.
    -   **Brick siding**: Keep weep holes open.
    -   **Header area**: Ideally, carry insulation **at least 150 mm (6 in.)** past header area. If not possible, air seal and insulate header area from interior.
5.  **Protect the exterior by covering the exposed insulation**:
    -   Protects from sunlight and physical damage.
    -   Covering from top of insulation to **at least 100 mm (4 in.) below settled ground level** (preferably **300 mm (12 in.)**).
    -   **Options**: Expanded metal lath with cement parging, polymer-modified pargings, pressure-treated plywood, vinyl/matching siding.
6.  **Refill the excavation**:
    -   Cover drain tiles with **150 mm (6 in.) of clean gravel (4 mm [1/8 in.] or larger)** and filter fabric. If drainage insulation is used, gravel extends **at least 100 mm (4 in.)** up insulation side.
    -   Backfill in stages, removing large objects, compacting ground.
    -   For poor draining soils, use free-draining backfill.
    -   Ensure ground slopes away from house (e.g., **10 percent slope or 200 mm [8 in.] for first 2 m [6 ft.]**).
    -   Add eavestroughs/downspouts to direct surface water **at least 2 m (6 ft.)** away.
    -   Cover excavation with patio stones, grass, or garden (wait for settling before expensive treatments).
7.  **Complete the finishing details**:
    -   **Windows**: Wrap insulation around foundation to meet frame, apply lath/parging, caulk joint. Sills should slope away.
    -   **Doors**: Outline with J-channel or flashing. May need to extend doorsill.
    -   **Penetrations**: Seal with compatible, flexible sealant to prevent wind, water, vermin entry.
8.  **Insulate the exterior joist header area**: If exterior insulation doesn't cover this area adequately, air seal and insulate from inside.

### 6.1.2 Complications

-   **Cold cellar or unheated garage**: Treat as exterior basement walls, insulate from inside, weatherstrip/insulate doorway, insulate ceiling. Extend outside insulation **at least 600 mm (24 in.)** beyond inside wall junction.
-   **Obstructions (concrete porch, paved driveway)**: Switch to inside insulation around obstacles. Allow **at least 600 mm (24 in.)** overlap to reduce thermal bridging.

## 6.2 Insulating the Basement from the Inside

**Considerations**: Structural problems, insulation requirements (type, RSI/R-value, location), wiring/plumbing upgrades, finishing details. Consult local building authorities for code compliance and specific regional issues (e.g., frost heave).

-   **Common insulation types**: Batt/blanket, glass fibre loose fill, polyurethane spray, rigid plastic board.
-   **Closed cell polyurethane spray foam**: High-quality, for uneven walls, helps control dampness, applied by certified installer.
-   **Rigid plastic board**: Higher RSI per mm than batt, takes less space.

### Safety Warning

-   Refer to Part 1.4 for general safety guidelines. Use powder-activated concrete fastening tools carefully, following directions.

### 6.2.1 How to Insulate Inside the Basement Using Only Rigid Board Insulation

-   **Best for**: Even, vertical basement walls (poured concrete, concrete block).
-   **Method**: Secure rigid, moisture-impermeable insulation panels to concrete with mechanical fastening strips, then protect with **12.7 mm ( in.) gypsum drywall**.
-   **Preparation**: Air seal all leakage paths (sill, penetrations) after wall checks/repairs.
-   **Insulation material**: Medium- to high-density foam board with good moisture resistance (extruded polystyrene, Type IV expanded polystyrene). Check manufacturer for foil-faced boards with cement/mortar.
-   **Installation**:
    -   Seal insulation to foundation with foam-compatible adhesive around perimeter.
    -   Mechanical fasteners are essential (wood fastening strips, or special clips/fasteners).
    -   Screw/nail drywall to strips secured to concrete with corrosion-resistant fasteners.
    -   Minimum **RSI 2.1 (R-12)**, or more as per local codes.
    -   Install in overlapping layers, fit snugly, use foam caulking and technical tape to seal.
-   **Finishing**: Insulate and seal joist area (see 6.2.6). Protect entire wall with **12.7 mm ( in.) gypsum board** or equivalent fire protection.

### 6.2.2 How to Frame and Insulate Inside the Basement

-   **Methods (three approaches)**:
    1.  Frame wall with single or double layer of batt insulation.
    2.  Frame wall filled with batt insulation and rigid board insulation backing.
    3.  Frame wall filled with batt insulation and spray foam backing.
-   **Preparation**: Air seal all leakage paths (sill, penetrations). Use dry lumber for framing (allow drying time if not dry). Refer to Section 3 (\"Materials\") and Section 4 (\"Comprehensive air leakage control\").

### 6.2.3 Frame Wall with Single or Double Layer of Batt Insulation

-   **Moisture protection**: Cover basement walls with **house wrap** (not plastic or tar paper). House wrap allows inward breathing/diffusion. Consult manufacturer for installation.
-   **House wrap installation**: Start at/just above grade, extend to basement floor and under bottom plate. Mechanically support with strapping (e.g., 1x3 lumber).
-   **Framing**:
    -   **Approach 1**: Wall close to (but not touching) house wrap (**12 mm [ in.] spacing**), using 2x4 or 2x6 lumber.
    -   **Approach 2**: Framing further from foundation, allowing batt insulation layer between frame and wall (more insulation, less thermal bridging, better moisture protection).
    -   Bottom plate on house wrap extension and continuous impermeable membrane (sill gasket).
    -   Top plate fastened to joists (may need nailing support if parallel).
    -   Square up walls.
    -   If expansive clay soils, may need **25 mm (1 in.) gap** between top plate and floor joists for movement.
    -   Install studs **600 mm (24 in.) on centre**.
    -   Extra framing around doors; single stud for non-load-bearing windows.
    -   Can build wall on floor and tilt into place if level/square/no obstructions.
    -   Install wiring/plumbing rough-ins.
-   **Insulating**:
    -   If space left behind frame, add horizontal batt insulation tight against foundation wall.
    -   Fill frame wall with vertical batt insulation, fitting snugly. No gaps, air spaces, or over-compression.
    -   For blown-in insulation, fill cavities to manufacturer's recommended density/RSI.
-   **Finishing**:
    -   Install **vapour barrier** over studs/insulation.
    -   **Polyethylene**: Suitable for proven dry basements.
    -   **Polyamide sheeting (smart barrier)**: Breathable membrane, changes water vapour permeance, allows wall to dry to interior. Follow manufacturer directions.
    -   **Airtight Drywall Approach (ADA)**: Uses rigid materials (drywall) thoroughly sealed with adhesive-backed foam tape and flexible caulking. Critical for sealing all connections.
        -   **ADA Sealing Considerations**: Airtight boxes for plumbing/drain lines, special airtight electrical boxes, caulk all wire penetrations, air seal window/door frames with expanding foam/caulking, foam gaskets/caulk for framing members in contact with floors/slabs/walls/ceiling, air seal obstructions (stairs), frame/air seal electrical panels, insulate/seal joist header space (6.2.6).
    -   Finishing must include **vapour barrier primer or paint**.

### 6.2.4 Frame Wall with Batt Insulation and Rigid Board Insulation

-   **Method**: Glue rigid board insulation to foundation, then cover with a frame wall incorporating batt insulation.
-   **Result**: Higher insulation, less interior space loss, good thermal bridging reduction, no additional moisture barrier needed.
-   **Best for**: Even, vertical basement walls (poured concrete, concrete block).
-   **Insulation material**: Moisture-resistant board (extruded polystyrene, Type IV expanded polystyrene).
-   **Preparation**: Same as 6.2.1.
-   **Installation**:
    -   Secure/seal rigid board insulation (**at least RSI 1.76 [R-10]**) to foundation with foam-compatible adhesive around perimeter.
    -   Air sealing creates an air/moisture barrier.
    -   Install wood-frame wall directly in contact with rigid board insulation (follow 6.2.3 techniques).
-   **Adding additional insulation**: Roughed-in wiring/plumbing, then insulated as in 6.2.3. Treat joist area (6.2.6).
-   **Finishing**: **Do not use polyethylene air/vapour barrier** (risk of double vapour barrier). Use smart barrier or ADA method. Exposed foam may need fire protection.

### 6.2.5 Frame Wall with Batt Insulation and Spray Foam Backing

-   **Method**: Build frame wall **25 to 50 mm (1 to 2 in.)** from foundation, then professional spray foam contractor applies **~50 mm (2 in.) of medium/high density polyurethane foam**.
-   **Benefits**: Fills/bonds to framing/wall, high insulation (approx. **RSI 0.98 [R-5.6/in.]**), reduces thermal bridging, provides moisture barrier.
-   **Limitation**: Higher cost.
-   **Preparation**: Discuss with contractor (address structural cracks, water leaks). Install wood frame wall as directed.
-   **Adding additional insulation**: After foam installation, rough in wiring/plumbing and insulate. Polyethylene vapour barrier not recommended.
-   **Finishing**: Same as 6.2.3.

### 6.2.6 Joist Header Space

-   **Description**: Also called rim joist space, foundation header space, or joist space. Area where floor joists meet foundation walls. Prone to air leakage, drafts, dust, pollen, vermin. Concrete block foundations can have additional leakage through center holes.
-   **Sealing**: Tops of concrete block foundations and other penetrations must be properly sealed.
-   **Joint configurations**:
    -   Floor joists on a sill plate.
    -   Floor joists partially embedded in the foundation.
    -   Floor joists fully embedded in the foundation.
-   **Insulating**: If exterior insulation doesn't cover header area, insulate/seal from inside.
-   **Air/Vapour Barrier**: Must be continuous for wall and header space if foundation walls insulated from interior.
-   **Fully-embedded joists**: Max **25 mm (1 in.) of foam board insulation** to avoid cold floors/damage.
-   **Building Codes**: Mandate insulation levels; check with local authorities.
-   **Polyurethane spray foam**: Excellent air sealing/insulation. Must be covered with fire-resistant material if basement ceiling is not installed.

### 6.2.7 Complications

-   **Wall space interrupted by pipes, ducts, electrical panel**:
    -   **Water lines**: Move away from wall or install insulation/vapour barrier behind pipes (warm side). Never in front. Penetrations through air/vapour barrier need plywood box sealed to barrier and caulked pipes.
    -   **Flue pipes**: Do not insulate around; check clearances with manufacturers/specialists. Do not insulate if proper clearances to furnaces/wood stoves cannot be maintained.
    -   **Electrical panels**: Be careful, panel is live even if house power is off. Have electrician help seal/move for new wall.
-   **Basement wall interrupted by a window**:
    -   Seal frame to wall with low-expansion spray foam caulk.
    -   Seal frames to vapour barrier system.

### 6.2.8 Rubble and Irregular Basement and Crawl Space Walls

-   **Characteristics**: Stone/rubble, rarely waterproofed outside.
-   **Exterior retrofit**: Always recommended.
-   **Interior insulation**: Possible if no water/moisture problems.
    -   **Method**: Cover interior wall with cement-based parging to smooth. Build frame wall, add max **RSI 2.1 (R-12) batt insulation**, finish as in 6.2.3.
    -   **Caution**: Avoid higher insulation values due to freeze-thaw risk. Check local authorities.
    -   **Closed-cell spray polyurethane foam**: Successfully reduces dampness, offers thermal protection. Max **RSI 2.1 (R-12)**. Must be installed by certified installer and covered with fire-resistant material.
-   **Very wet basements**: If prone to flooding/high moisture, may be best not to insulate walls. Consult building authorities/contractor to treat floor joist space as an exposed/insulated floor.
-   **Additional modifications**: For plumbing and heating systems may be needed.

### 6.2.9 Part of the Basement is a Cold Cellar or an Unheated Garage

-   **Method**: Apply insulation to the separating wall as if it were an external wall. Weatherstrip/insulate doorway. Insulate ceiling (see 6.4). Overlap insulation as in Figure 6-11.
-   **Cold cellar temperature**: Monitor winter temperature to prevent freezing. Insulate ceiling if room is too warm or floor above is cold.

### 6.2.10 Basement Pony Walls

-   **Description**: Short wood frame wall on top of a conventional concrete foundation.
-   **Insulation**: Wood frame part insulated between studs. Concrete part insulated on interior (assuming no moisture problems). Concrete insulation extends **~200 mm (8 in.)** to overlap frame part, creating a ledge.
-   **Alternative**: Install frame wall from floor to ceiling, filling cavity with insulation (higher thermal performance, but deep window frame).

### 6.2.11 Overhangs

-   **Method**: Seal and insulate floors that overhang foundation.
    -   Remove finish underneath, air seal space between joists with polyurethane foam or caulked rigid insulation.
    -   Insulate joist space with batt/blanket insulation, add air barrier, then re-install finish.
    -   **Rigid board insulation**: Can be applied to underside of overhang to reduce thermal bridging (still air seal/insulate joist space).
    -   **Spray foam/dense-pack fibre**: Can be used in cavities.
    -   **Warmer floor option**: Airtight cavity with small air gap between insulation top and flooring bottom (allows convection, but decreases insulation in joist space; offset by adding rigid insulation below).

### 6.2.12 Basement Floors

-   **General**: Seldom insulated as most heat loss is through walls.
-   **For comfort, moisture control, radon reduction**: Apply a moisture barrier to the floor or seal floor to prevent moisture accumulation.
-   **Floor drainage system**: May be installed under new floor.
-   **New/replacement concrete slab**: Excellent opportunity for in-floor heating or radiant-ready installation. Consult heating contractor.

### 6.2.13 Uninhabited Basements and Crawl Spaces

-   **Method**: Medium- and high-density polyurethane foams applied directly to poured concrete, concrete block, brick, or rubble walls.
-   **Requirement**: Sprayed with a non-flammable covering to meet local code requirements.

## 6.3 Crawl Spaces

-   **Insulation methods (two ways)**:
    1.  Insulate crawl space walls (creates a heated area).
    2.  Insulate house floor above (keeps heat out of crawl space).
-   **Heated crawl spaces**: Treat as short basements; renovate as described in this chapter.
-   **General**: Fix water leaks, remove water infiltration sources. **Never vent clothes dryer into a crawl space**.
-   **Insulating walls vs. ceiling**: Insulating walls is generally recommended to avoid insulating/protecting pipes/heating systems. Easier than ceiling in tight spaces, requires fewer materials.
-   **Moisture barrier on floor**: Minimum **0.10 mm (4 mil) clear or opaque polyethylene**, overlapped, caulked, taped at seams. Mechanically fasten to walls/obstructions, seal to wall moisture barrier. Use boards/scrap to hold down (not sand/gravel).

### 6.3.1 How to Insulate a Heated Crawl Space

#### From the Outside

-   **Method**: Same as outside basement wall (see 6.1).
-   **Obstructions**: If outside obstructions prevent full encirclement, insulate inside portion of wall. Overlap interior/exterior insulation coverage by **at least 600 mm (24 in.)**.
-   **Ventilation**: If not open to full basement, should have ventilation (not exterior vents due to condensation risk). Incorporate whole-house ventilation or use dehumidifier. Permanently seal existing vents if space will be heated.
-   **Shallow footings**: If above frost line, insulate outside walls. Place horizontal insulation sloping away from foundation to keep footings warm.

#### From the Inside

-   **Method**: Same as inside basement (rigid board or spray foam, see 6.2). Treat joist spaces as in 6.2.2. Check with building inspector for fire protective coverings if foam insulation is used.
-   **Moisture barrier**: Apply polyethylene moisture barrier to floor. Ensure adequate ventilation (as above).

### 6.3.2 How to Insulate a Partially Heated Crawl Space

-   **Method**: Insulate between and under joists (can lead to freezing pipes, frozen ground, rot). Use floor insulation **only when combined with foundation-wall insulation**.
-   **Insulation board under joists**: Reduces thermal bridging, increases floor insulation. If cavity airtight, air gap between insulation top and flooring bottom can create warmer floor (trade-off with insulation value).
-   **Protective covering**: Check local building code for exposed insulation board.

#### Points of General Importance

-   **Air and vapour barrier**: On the warm (top) side of insulation. If floor already impermeable (vinyl, linoleum, plywood), it serves as barrier. Seal all air leaks (piping, wiring holes). Rim joist area air tightness is critical.
-   **Batt insulation**: Held in place with breathable building wrap, chicken wire, polystyrene bead board (Type I or II), or commercial support system.
-   **Placement**: Tightly against underside of floor above, filling joist cavity.
-   **Ducts and pipes**: Tape seams in heating ducts, insulate all ducts and water pipes. Even insulated water pipes can freeze; consider energy-efficient, self-regulating electric heat trace.
-   **Ventilation**: Adequately ventilate in spring (**1 to 500 vent area to floor area ratio**). Plug/insulate vents in winter. Inspect/monitor humidity monthly.
-   **Moisture barrier**: Must be on crawl space floor.
-   **Basement/crawl space separation**: Insulate wall separating them if basement floor is insulated.
-   **Frost heave**: Risk if ground level inside is lower than outside. Keep water away from foundation walls.
-   **Safety precaution**: Install thermostat attached to small heater to prevent freezing.

## 6.4 Open Foundations

-   **Description**: Older homes, cottages, modular/mobile homes with exposed floors.
-   **Insulation**: Between joists, similar to crawl spaces. Vapour barrier on warm side (above insulation).
-   **Requirements**: Good air seal, protection from wind, insects, animals.
-   **Insulated skirt**: Can be built around foundation to create a heated crawl space.
-   **Modular/mobile homes**: Floor may have bagged insulation. Seal any perforations/openings, especially around service lines (water, sewer, gas/oil, combustion air inlet).
-   **Existing covered joist space**: Consider dense-pack fibre insulation (no air space) or medium/high-density foam as vapour barrier, air barrier, and primary insulation.
-   **Clothes dryer**: **Do not vent into floor space**.

## 6.5 Concrete Slab on Grade

-   **Insulation**: Applied exactly as outside of a full basement (see 6.1).
-   **Frost-susceptible soils**: Install horizontal layer of impermeable insulation in soil at a **slope ratio of 1:5 for at least 1 m (3 ft.)**.
-   **Dry concrete slab**: Helps limit mould, water damage, musty odours. Perform dampness test (described at beginning of chapter) even if slab appears dry.
