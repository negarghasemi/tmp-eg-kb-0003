# Energy Efficiency Report: Best Practices in Heat Pumps

This document summarizes best practices in heat pump sizing, selection, and installation, drawing insights from various case studies across Canada. It highlights approaches for HVAC contractors, energy advisors, builders, and renovators to achieve cost-effective, comfortable, and energy-efficient homes with significant utility and greenhouse gas (GHG) savings.

## I. Key Themes and General Recommendations

### 1. The \"Envelope First\" Approach

*   **Prioritize Building Envelope Upgrades**: Before investing in new HVAC systems, focus on improving the building's thermal envelope. This includes:
    *   **Air Sealing / Draft Proofing**: Often the measure with the quickest return on investment.
    *   **Insulation**: Upgrade attic, roof, basement, crawlspace, and wall insulation (e.g., continuous spray foam, exterior finish insulation systems).
    *   **Windows and Doors**: Upgrade to high-performance, low-emissivity, triple-glazed units.
*   **Benefits**: Optimizing the envelope first significantly reduces the heating load, allowing for the installation of smaller, more efficient heat pump systems and minimizing reliance on supplementary heating. This leads to lower capital costs, quicker payback, and greater energy and GHG savings.

### 2. Right-Sizing HVAC Systems

*   **Avoid Rule-of-Thumb Sizing**: Traditional sizing methods often lead to grossly oversized systems (50-80% too large), resulting in short cycling, uneven heating, and higher costs.
*   **Accurate Heat Load Calculations**: Utilize energy models (e.g., Hot 2000) and tools like NRCans Air-source Heat Pump Sizing and Selection Guide. Data from EnerGuide home evaluations (air tightness, insulation levels, window/door types) is crucial for precise calculations.
*   **Smaller Systems**: A well-insulated, air-sealed home can be comfortably heated by a significantly smaller heat pump (e.g., a 2.5-ton unit replacing a 6-ton furnace).
*   **Benefits**: Prevents unnecessary electrical backup, reduces energy costs, improves comfort, and avoids costly electrical service upgrades.

### 3. Electrical System Considerations

*   **Electrical Panel Upgrades**: Adding new electric loads (heat pumps, EV chargers, induction cooktops) can trigger the need to upgrade electrical panels (e.g., from 100 amp to 200 amp service). These upgrades can cost **$3,000 to $12,000** and are often not covered by rebates.
*   **Strategic Planning**: Proactive planning and detailed electrical load analysis can often avoid unnecessary electrical panel upgrades for routine heat pump retrofits.
*   **Load Control Options**: Utilize load control devices and strategies allowed by electrical codes to manage peak power demand and stay within existing service capacity.
*   **Future-Proofing**: For homes with multiple electrification goals (e.g., heat pumps, EV charging, solar, battery storage), a larger service upgrade might be justifiable for long-term vision and resiliency.
*   **Smart Panels**: New technologies and smart panel tools enable sophisticated load management.

### 4. Optimal Heat Pump Operation and Controls

*   **Advanced Control Strategies**: Properly configure heat pump controls to maintain thermal and economic balance points.
    *   **Thermal Balance Point**: Outdoor temperature where heat pump output matches building's heating load.
    *   **Economic Balance Point**: Considers utility costs to determine the most cost-effective switchover point between the heat pump and backup heating.
*   **Minimize Backup Heat Reliance**: Limit the use of supplementary electric or fossil fuel backup heating to only when absolutely necessary (e.g., below -20 to -30 Celsius or 5% of the heating season).
*   **Homeowner Education**: Educate residents on optimal thermostat operation (e.g., steady temperature settings, avoiding frequent on/off cycling) to maximize heat pump efficiency and avoid triggering expensive backup systems.
*   **Continuous Operation**: Heat pumps operate most efficiently when running continuously at lower outputs rather than cycling on and off.

### 5. Professional Collaboration and Training

*   **Certified Professionals**: Work with certified energy evaluators, energy advisors, HVAC contractors, and licensed electricians.
*   **Comprehensive Assessments**: Invest in thorough energy audits and site visits to understand the home's unique characteristics and heating/cooling requirements.
*   **Contractor Expertise**: Contractors need training on heat pump installations, airflow measurements, and understanding existing distribution systems.
*   **Data-Driven Quoting**: Use tools and data (like NRCan's guide) to provide objective comparisons and demonstrate the business case for heat pumps to customers.

## II. Case Study Highlights and Specific Outcomes

### 1. Net Zero All Electric Homes  A Builders Journey (Westport, Ontario)

*   **Project**: LandArk Homes building net-zero energy-ready homes using heat pumps.
*   **Challenge**: No natural gas available.
*   **Solutions**: Super insulation (continuous spray foam, triple glaze windows), super tight building envelope (aerosol-based sealing), and super ventilation (optimized HVAC).
*   **Performance**: Homes achieve excellent EnerGuide ratings, with no drafts and even temperatures.
*   **Heat Loss Reduction**: From ~60,000 BTU (conventional) to ~24,000 BTU.
*   **Savings**: Energy bills reduced by 50%.
*   **Challenges**: Initial resistance from trades, complexity of HVAC sizing for tight envelopes, lack of initial market demand.

### 2. Sizing Supplementary Heating Systems in Heat Pump Retrofits

*   **Project**: Retrofit of 11 townhouses with weatherstripping, insulation, and cold climate air source heat pumps.
*   **Goal**: Limit reliance on backup heat and avoid electrical panel upgrades (costs: $3,000-$10,000).
*   **Strategy**: Reduced building's heat loss by **70%** through envelope upgrades.
*   **Outcome**: Enabled smaller heat pumps and a small 3KW supplementary heater, operating only ~5% of the heating season, successfully avoiding electrical service upgrades.

### 3. Heat Pump Controls for Net-Zero in Colleges and Universities (Fanshawe College)

*   **Project**: Retrofit of 66 townhouses at Kestrel Court.
*   **Goal**: 30% GHG reduction by 2030, 80% by 2050.
*   **Strategy**: Envelope upgrades first, then right-sizing HVAC and advanced control strategies.
*   **Controls**: Use of Thermal and Economic Balance Point temperatures to optimize heat pump operation and limit natural gas backup.
*   **Outcome**: Heat pumps with appropriate controls reduce operating costs and carbon footprint.

### 4. Reducing Heating Loads to Optimize Heat Pump Retrofits (Fanshawe College)

*   **Project**: Modernization of 11 townhouse units.
*   **Initial State**: Leakage rate of 5 air changes/hour (compared to best-in-class <1.5).
*   **Key Measures**: Air sealing (quickest ROI), attic insulation, triple-glazed windows, basement/wall insulation. Used Exterior Finish Insulation System (EFIS).
*   **Mechanical**: Replaced dryer/stove vents with Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV) units.
*   **Outcome**: Achieved **70% energy and GHG reduction** by prioritizing envelope upgrades and right-sizing systems.

### 5. Electrifying Homes with Heat Pumps (B.C. Homeowner)

*   **Goal**: Decarbonize home, transition off natural gas, reach net-zero.
*   **Existing Electrical**: 100 amp panel with 56 amp peak load (80 amp limit).
*   **Heat Pump Sizing**: Home heat load ~30,000 BTUs/hour, installed a 2.5-ton cold climate heat pump (max 34 amps, 40 amp breaker).
*   **Decision**: Upgraded to 200 amp service due to future plans (greenhouse, solar, battery storage), despite initial 100 amp being potentially sufficient for just the heat pump.
*   **Innovation**: Implemented a split panel solution for essential services and load management.

### 6. Efficient Heating Upgrade in Manufactured Homes (Campbell River, B.C.)

*   **Challenge**: Manufactured homes typically have poor insulation, high air leakage, and high heating costs ($300-400/month).
*   **Solution**: BC Hydro pilot program offering free heat pump installations.
*   **Heat Pump Efficiency**: Heat pumps use 2-3 times less electricity than electric resistance heating.
*   **Benefits**: Reduces demand on power grid, stabilizes infrastructure costs.
*   **Outcome**: Utility bills dropped by **30-40%** ($85-$120/month) in the first heating season. Improved comfort and heat distribution.

### 7. Oil Furnace to Heat Pump Retrofits (Halifax, Nova Scotia)

*   **Motivation**: Transition from oil heating due to cost, environmental impact, and insurance concerns.
*   **Initial State**: Home heat loss ~26,000 BTUs/hour. Existing oil furnace 72,000 BTUs/hour (3 times oversized).
*   **Process**: Energy audit identified insulation and air leakage issues. Airflow measurements determined existing ductwork could support a 3-ton heat pump (previously assumed to be too small).
*   **Cost Analysis**: Capital cost $17,500, offset by $5,000 rebate. Avoided furnace/AC replacement costs of $7,500. Net incremental cost $5,000.
*   **Savings**: Utility bills $50 lower on average annually. Reduced CO2 emissions by 1.5 tonnes in the first year.
*   **Benefits**: Increased comfort (stable temperatures), reduced GHG emissions, proactive equipment replacement.

## III. Financial and Environmental Impact

*   **Cost Savings**: Heat pump retrofits, especially when combined with envelope upgrades, lead to significant reductions in utility bills (e.g., 30-50% less energy consumption, $50-$120/month savings).
*   **GHG Reduction**: Heat pumps contribute to substantial cuts in greenhouse gas emissions (e.g., 70% energy and GHG reduction, 1.5 tonnes CO2 reduction annually).
*   **Incentives**: Government incentives and loan programs (e.g., NRCan's 10-year interest-free loan) make heat pump installations more financially viable.
*   **Grid Management**: Switching to more efficient electric heating reduces strain on power grids, allowing utilities to stabilize infrastructure costs and free up capacity for other electrification efforts.
