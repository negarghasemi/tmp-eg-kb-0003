# Keeping The Heat In - Section 5: Roofs and Attics

This section focuses on improving the energy efficiency of roofs and attics through insulation, air sealing, and ventilation. Attics are often a starting point for homeowners to enhance energy performance, but proper air sealing and ventilation are crucial to prevent heat loss and moisture-related issues.

## 5.1 General Considerations for All Attics
Before starting work, thoroughly inspect the attic. This includes examining accessibility, assessing conditions, checking existing barriers, and considering structural elements.

### Accessibility
- Most accessible attics have an interior ceiling hatch. Ensure it's large enough for materials.
- Exterior entries (e.g., in a gable end) simplify air sealing and provide firefighter access.
- Vertical clearances less than 1m (39 in.) restrict free movement.

### Assessments
- **Moisture Problems**: Check roof framing, sheathing, and fascia for leaks, stains, mould, flaking, or rot. Correct moisture issues before insulating.
    - Moisture can originate from roof/flashing failures (e.g., around chimneys, ice dam leaks) or from inside the house via air leakage.
    - Discoloration of insulation is a sign of air leakage.
    - Check for frost buildup during cold snaps (heavy buildup indicates ventilation or air leakage issues).
    - Inspect during rainstorms to differentiate internal vs. external moisture sources.
- **Existing Insulation**: Examine type, condition (dry, wet, compact), average depth, and coverage.
    - Remove damaged or contaminated insulation (mould, vermin).
    - *Do not disturb vermiculite insulation due to health concerns.*
    - If wet, dry it and remove moisture source before covering.
    - Ensure even distribution and full depth coverage, especially around the perimeter.
- **Existing Air and Vapour Barriers**: Locate and assess the condition of existing barriers.
    - An air barrier must be continuous; repair holes/tears and seal penetrations.
    - Pull back insulation to find and seal air leaks.
    - *Again, do not disturb vermiculite insulation.*
    - Increased insulation makes the attic colder, increasing condensation risk if air sealing is poor.
    - If no air/vapour barrier exists, focus on comprehensive air sealing with caulking, gaskets, and weatherstripping.

### Structural Considerations
- When renovating, consider upgrading the roof structure for future photovoltaics or solar hot water panels.
- Adding shingle layers increases roof load; removing old shingles is generally recommended.

### 5.1.2 Fire and Other Hazards in Attics
**Safety warning**: *Address fire and health hazards before air sealing and insulating.*

- **Masonry Chimneys**:
    - Inspect for charring, soot, crumbling masonry/mortar, or deteriorating lining. Repair any issues before insulating.
    - Air seal gaps with sheet metal flashing and non-combustible sealant.
    - Construct a barrier 75 mm (3 in.) from the chimney face and 75 mm (3 in.) higher than the insulation to prevent contact.
- **Metal Chimneys**:
    - Maintain a minimum 75 mm (3 in.) clearance from insulation, or as specified by the manufacturer.
    - Install a non-flammable radiation shield with a 75 mm (3 in.) air space.
    - Air seal the fire stop to the air/vapour barrier with non-combustible sealant.
    - The radiation shield should extend 75 mm (3 in.) above insulation; use a clamp-type storm collar at the top.
- **Electrical Installations**:
    - Work safely (turn off power). Consult an electrician for questionable wiring or corroded connections.
- **Recessed Lighting**:
    - Standard recessed lights are hard to air seal and can be fire hazards if covered.
    - Replace with approved airtight fixtures or covers, or create a barrier to keep insulation away.
    - Consider LED flush/flat mount fixtures for better efficiency and easier sealing.
- **Animal Droppings**:
    - If significant, *do not disturb* due to potential airborne moulds, parasite eggs, and bacteria. See Part 1.4 for health and safety considerations.

### 5.1.3 Air Sealing
Seal the following areas comprehensively:
- Around **plumbing stacks and pipes**: Use flexible gaskets or polyethylene sleeves sealed to plywood collars.
- Around **wires or ceiling light fixtures** that penetrate the attic floor and wall plates.
- Around **ducting** entering the attic: Seal joints with aluminum tape or mastic, seal gaps where ducts penetrate the ceiling. Ducts should be below insulation or wrapped and protected.
- At the junction of **ceiling and interior wall partitions**: Caulk cracks along interior walls.
- At the top of **interior and exterior walls**: Block open wall cavities with rigid board insulation and caulk. Seal cracked or poorly fitted top plates.
- Around **attic hatches**.
- Around the **chimney**: Building codes require non-combustible fire stops for air spaces.
- Along the edge of **shared walls**: Ensure gaps between party walls and the attic floor are well sealed. Hire a contractor for concrete block party walls.

### 5.1.4 Ventilation
Attic ventilation reduces summer heat buildup, prolongs roofing life, decreases AC loads, and defends against moisture. It also helps prevent ice dams.
- Ensure existing vents are clear of insulation or debris.
- **Ventilation Ratio**: For peaked roofs, aim for a 1:300 ratio of unobstructed vent area to insulated ceiling area. Increase vent area if screens or baffles are present.
- Position vents for good cross-ventilation (end-to-end, top-to-bottom).
- Vents alone do not prevent condensation or solve air leakage problems.
- Building codes require minimum attic ventilation, even with airtight ceilings.
- *Do not automatically increase ventilation.* Avoid electric exhaust fans, as they can draw house air into the attic, increasing heat loss and moisture.
- Wind-driven roof vents are no more effective than capped vents.
- A mixture of vent types and locations often works best.

## 5.2 Easily Accessible Attics
After inspection and remedial work, prioritize air and moisture control.

### 5.2.1 Air and Moisture Control
Five options for installing an air barrier in an unfinished attic:
1.  **Concentrate on air sealing** (most practical).
2.  Install polyethylene over the top of the joists.
3.  Install polyethylene between the joists.
4.  Spray foam between the joists (requires a contractor).
5.  Spray foam under the roof to create a conditioned attic (requires a contractor).

### 5.2.2 Concentrate on Air Sealing
- Paint the room side of the ceiling with two coats of oil-based paint or latex vapour barrier paint for vapour inhibition.
- Seal all air leaks into the attic (refer to Section 4).
- **Install polyethylene over joists**: Suitable for unobstructed attics. Attach polyethylene to joist tops with caulking and staples. Seal obvious air leaks first. Install at least twice the insulating value over the polyethylene (e.g., 1/3, 2/3 rule) to prevent moisture trapping.
    - Difficult areas like eaves can be sealed with spray foam or rigid board insulation.
- **Install polyethylene between joists**: Labor-intensive method for truss roofs. Remove existing insulation, cut foam boards to fit, caulk all edges/gaps/joints. Seal around obstructions like electrical wires.
- **Spray foam between joists**: Contractors apply closed-cell foam for air sealing and insulation. Requires removal of existing insulation/dust. Add minimum 50 mm (2 in.), then top up with other insulation.
- **Spray foam under the roof (Hot Roof)**: Closed-cell spray foam applied directly under the roof surface, eliminating roof venting. Attic becomes a conditioned space. Requires building inspector approval and certified contractor.

### 5.2.3 Installing Insulation
Common materials: batt/blanket or loose-fill insulation.
- **Batt or Blanket Insulation**:
    - Fit snugly between ceiling joists without compression. Cut to fit non-standard joist spacing.
    - Butt batt ends together.
    - Cover exterior wall top plate but do not block venting. Maintain 38-50 mm (1.5-2 in.) space between insulation and roof sheathing using baffles.
    - Insulate above and below cross bracing, avoiding compression.
    - Install the first layer to fill joist space, then a second layer perpendicular to block heat flow through joists.
    - Fill gaps with batt pieces or loose fill.
- **Loose-fill Insulation**:
    - Can be poured or blown in. Use depth indicators to ensure even depth.
    - Fill all nooks and crannies.
    - Prevent insulation from blocking eave ventilation by installing rigid board or wood baffles.
    - Extend insulation over the exterior wall top.
    - Build a framework around the attic hatch to contain insulation.
    - Calculate required bags based on desired RSI value and bag coverage.
    - *Remember to seal the attic hatch and insulate up to party walls.*
    - *Do not cover recessed light fixtures unless approved.*

### 5.2.4 Ventilation
- **Ideal Setup**: 50% continuous soffit vents, 50% gable, ridge, or roof vents. Ridge vents are preferable if practical, with baffles to prevent wind/snow entry.
- If only soffit vents exist, add gable end, ridge, or roof vents for cross-ventilation.

## 5.3 Houses with Half Storeys
These attics can be difficult to access and insulate.

### 5.3.1 Air and Vapour Control
- Follow directions for accessible attics. 
- **Critical Air Leakage Point**: Seal ceiling joists immediately beneath knee walls flush with the finish, using foam board and caulk/spray foam.
- Dense-pack loose-fill insulation can be used between floorboards if an air barrier is present.

### 5.3.2 Ventilation
- Ventilate attic spaces above and beside the top storey separately using gable vents.
- Alternatively, use gable vents/ridge vent for the top attic and soffit vents for side areas. Ensure ventilation space in rafter sections.
- Install rafter vents if necessary for airflow between insulated areas.

### 5.3.3 Insulation
- Spray foam and rigid foam are options, but batts are often easier in confined spaces.
- Keep electrical outlets and pipes on the warm side of the air/vapour barrier and insulation.
- Treat the attic floor behind the knee wall and the attic space above the half storey's ceiling like a standard unfinished attic, with added air sealing under the knee wall.
- Insulate end walls as described in Section 7.
- Treat the knee wall like an unfinished attic floor (vertically), using polyethylene strips or painting a vapour barrier. Secure insulation with building paper, cardboard, or rigid board.
- Sloping ceiling portion can be filled with insulation if penetrations are sealed and local codes permit (hot roof concept).
- Exposed foam in accessible areas must be fire-protected.
- Consider adding rigid board directly to the ceiling and covering with drywall to increase comfort, though it may reduce headroom.
- High-density blown insulation can reduce airflow and improve thermal values.
- **Dormer Windows**: Insulate dormer walls with batts. Consider dense-pack blown-in insulation and rigid board to reduce thermal bridging due to many framing members.
- **Floorboards**: If the attic is floored, lift boards to insulate as an unfinished attic. Check for wiring/recessed lights; protect or remove heat sources.
    - Alternatively, contractors can dense-pack loose-fill insulation through access holes. Seal air leaks first.
    - Adding insulation on top of floorboards helps keep them warm and reduces thermal bridging.

## 5.4 Cramped Attics, Cathedral Ceilings and Flat Roofs
These areas present challenges for insulation and ventilation.

### 5.4.1 Cramped Attics
Options for insulating cramped attics:
1.  Insulate on top of the existing roof (see Part 5.5).
2.  Add rigid board and drywall to the ceiling (if headroom allows).
3.  Hire a contractor for dense-pack loose-fill insulation.

- If no new sheet-material air barrier can be installed, ensure no moisture problems, reasonable humidity levels, and all ceiling air leaks are sealed. Painting the ceiling with vapour barrier paint adds protection.
- Contractors must prevent insulation from blocking eaves ventilation.
- Precautions are needed for heat sources like recessed lights and chimneys.
- Ventilation can be difficult. Soffit vents with roof vents or built-up ridge vents may work if the roof extends past exterior walls.
- For houses lacking eaves, carefully seal the ceiling from below and insulate without additional vents.
- Monitor for heavy frost buildup, which indicates a need for venting, air sealing, and humidity reduction.

### 5.4.2 Cathedral Ceilings and Flat Roofs
These require qualified contractors due to limited space for insulation and ventilation.
- **Renovation Options**: Rigid board insulation directly under the ceiling, covered with new finish. Discuss vapour barrier installation with a contractor.
- **Dense-packing**: Existing space between ceiling and roof can be dense-packed with cellulose or glass fibre by a contractor (56-72 kg/m or 3.5-4.5 lb./cu. ft.). This eliminates roof ventilation and requires comprehensive air sealing.
- **Air Sealing**: Take extra care to seal air leaks into the ceiling from below, especially around wiring, plumbing, and open partition walls.

## 5.5 Ice Dams
Ice dams are large ice masses on roof edges and gutters, causing water backup and leaks.

### 5.5.1 Roof Type and Ice Dams
- Occur when attic temperature is above freezing, melting snow on the roof, which then refreezes at colder overhangs.
- Well-sealed and insulated attics result in cool roofs and generally do not have ice dams.
- Complicated roofs with many valleys, dormers, or large overhangs are more prone to ice damming.

### 5.5.2 Locating Areas Prone to Ice Dams
- Look for areas where snow melts first after a frost or light snow (e.g., horizontal melt line on 1.5-storey houses, under exhaust fans, plumbing vents, skylights, leaky attic hatches).
- **Best prevention**: Seal all attic air leaks and insulate thoroughly.
- Dark wall siding can contribute by creating warm convection currents; sealing soffit vents may help.

### 5.5.3 Dealing with Restricted Attic Space
- Limited space at eaves for adequate insulation can promote ice damming.
- Use high-performance insulation (e.g., spray foam, extruded polystyrene) at the edges.
- Install a piece of extruded polystyrene 25 mm (1 in.) from the sheathing to maintain ventilation air space, then fill the remaining space with insulation.

### 5.5.4 Cathedral Ceilings
- Ice dams are more difficult to resolve due to access.
- Principles of prevention (air sealing, insulation, ventilation) still apply.
- Best dealt with by contractors specializing in dense-pack insulation or during re-roofing.

### 5.5.5 Installing a New Roof
- An opportunity to air seal, insulate, and install an ice-shield membrane.
- A well-sealed roof needs minimal ventilation. If uncertain about sealing effectiveness, maintain a ventilation channel from soffit to peak.
- Self-sealing membranes under shingles prevent leaks but do not stop ice dams.

### 5.5.6 Quick Fixes and Their Limitations
- **Electric heating cables**: Melt channels but use significant energy and are unsightly.
- **Removing gutters**: Prevents ice traps but directs roof water to the foundation.
- **Physical ice removal**: Damages roof surfaces.
- **Snow removal**: Helps but can be difficult or unsafe.

**Preferred Solution**: Keep house heat out of the attic through air sealing and insulation.

## 5.6 Renovations and Repair
Renovations offer a chance to ensure a well-insulated attic with an effective air and vapour barrier.

### Converting Unfinished Attics to Living Space
- Install collar ties for structural support for the roof and new ceiling.
- Ensure continuous thermal envelope: insulation in rafter spaces fits snugly against walls, no gaps at the attic floor perimeter.
- Leave clear space between insulation and roof sheathing for ventilation. Seal roof vents in unheated attic parts.
- Staple a continuous polyethylene air/vapour barrier to rafters and studs, sealing edges and seams.
- Add strapping (2x2 or 2x3) perpendicular to rafters/studs for more insulation, filling cavities and covering with a continuous, sealed air/vapour barrier.
- Alternatively, secure rigid board insulation (25-50 mm) to rafters/studs, seal/install a vapour barrier, and cover with drywall.
- Insulation between collar ties: Apply from below with a continuous air/vapour barrier. More insulation can be added if access allows.

### 5.6.1 Dropped Ceiling
An excellent option for thermally upgrading cathedral ceilings or flat roofs, especially during renovations.
- **Options**:
    - Construct a new ceiling immediately below the existing one. Install batts or rigid insulation, followed by a continuous air/vapour barrier.
    - Extend existing rafters or trusses to accommodate additional insulation using cross strapping or plywood gussets.
- It's advisable to remove the interior finish to perform air sealing and check existing insulation/vapour barrier.
- Mechanically fasten rigid board insulation directly to the existing ceiling structure. Fit boards tightly and seal seams for an air barrier. Extend electrical fixtures to accommodate the new ceiling depth.

### 5.6.2 Adding a New Roof
Suitable for cathedral ceilings and flat roofs during major exterior alterations.
- **Method**: Install rigid board insulation on top of the existing roof. Use sheet polyethylene or an elastomer membrane as an air/vapour barrier, following the 1/3  2/3 rule. Alternatively, use closed-cell spray foam or low-permeability rigid board with taped joints.
- Insulate and air seal spaces between joists along the roof perimeter to prevent heat escape. Line blocking material edges with the exterior wall finish and caulk seams.
- Alternatively, build up the existing roof or frame a new one, filling with batt or loose-fill insulation. Ensure a tightly sealed air/vapour barrier. This may add structural load and must comply with local codes.
- Use new trusses or purlins to span the roof length. Install new rafters, proper insulation to avoid air movement and thermal bridging, and leave clear space for ventilation.
