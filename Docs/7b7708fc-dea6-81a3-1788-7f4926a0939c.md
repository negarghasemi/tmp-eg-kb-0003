# Keeping The Heat In - Section 8: Upgrading windows and exterior doors

Windows, doors, and skylights can account for up to **35 percent of total house heat loss**. This section focuses on upgrading these components to save energy.

## 8.1 Windows

Options for improving window energy efficiency include repairs, retrofits, or full replacement. If frames are in good condition, **inserts** (new sash and glazing units) can be a good option, especially for heritage homes. The best choice for replacement is **ENERGY STAR certified windows** or high-performance inserts.

### 8.1.1 Window types

Residential windows and skylights are either **operable** (casement, awning, hopper, tilt, turn, slider) or **non-operable (fixed)**. Operable windows allow for ventilation or emergency escape, while non-operable ones may be more airtight.

### 8.1.2 Material options

Common materials include aluminum, fiberglass, vinyl, and wood, or combinations. The **overall energy efficiency** is more dependent on the product's design rather than just the material, as reflected in the energy-performance rating.

### 8.1.3 Glazing

Glazing refers to the transparent material (usually glass) in a window, door, or skylight. Options include:

-   **Glazing layers**: Products can be single-, double-, triple-, or quad-glazed. More layers reduce outdoor noise. Triple-glazed units may substitute one pane with a thin polyester film to reduce weight.
-   **Low-E coatings**: Fine, transparent metal coatings applied to glazing surfaces. They **reduce heat loss in winter and heat gain in summer by up to 50%**. This helps maintain comfortable indoor temperatures, reduces HVAC load, and saves energy.
    -   **Passive low-E**: High Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC), harnesses sun's energy for heating.
    -   **Solar control low-E**: Lower SHGC, reduces air conditioning load.
    -   *After-market low-E films* can also be applied to existing units.
-   **Gas fill**: Odourless, colourless, inert gases (e.g., argon or krypton) fill the space(s) between glazings to **reduce heat transfer**.
-   **Insulated spacers**: Spacers made with thermal breaks (foam, plastic, composites) reduce edge heat loss and condensation problems compared to traditional aluminum-box spacers.
-   **Optimal glazing spacing**: The ideal spacing between glazings to minimize convection losses is **12 to 16 mm (1/2 to 5/8 in.)**.

### 8.1.4 Rating information

Standardized testing helps rank the energy performance of windows and skylights. Key terminology includes:

-   **U-factor**: Measures the rate of **heat loss** of a window assembly. **Lower U-factor indicates greater resistance to heat loss**.
    -   Better double-glazed windows: **1.2 W/mK or lower**.
    -   Triple-glazed windows: **as low as 0.8 W/mK**.
-   **Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC)**: A fraction (between 0 and 1) indicating the amount of the suns heat that can pass through. 
    -   **Lower SHGC**: Less solar heat transmitted, **reduces cooling load**.
    -   **Higher SHGC**: More solar energy allowed, **reduces heating load**.
    -   **Optimal SHGC for energy-efficient/new homes in Canada: 0.3-0.4**.
    -   **High SHGC (greater than 0.4)** is suitable for:
        -   Homes built before 1980 or not well insulated/air sealed.
        -   Sunny areas with short cooling seasons (less than two months, summer temperatures < 25C).
        -   Homes with passive solar heating design.
-   **Energy Rating (ER)**: A unitless number reflecting the balance between heat transfer (U-factor) and solar gain. **Higher numbers indicate slower heat transfer without significantly reducing solar gain**.
-   **Visible Transmittance (VT)**: The percentage of visible light that can pass through the glazing. Higher VT means more visible light.
-   **Centre-of-Glass U-factor**: Only considers the glass portion, which can make a product appear more energy-efficient than its overall assembly.

### 8.1.5 Replacing glazing, sashes and windows

Properly installed energy-efficient windows enhance comfort by reducing drafts and increasing interior surface temperature, thus reducing condensation. Features of energy-efficient windows include:

-   Double-, triple- or even quadruple-glazing
-   Low-emissivity (*low-E*) glass
-   Inert gas, such as argon or krypton, in the sealed unit
-   Low conductivity or *warm-edge* spacer bars
-   Insulated frames and sashes
-   Good air tightness

If glazing is single-pane or in poor condition, consider a **new sealed glazing window insert**. If the frame is poor, replace the entire unit.

### 8.1.6 Taking stock

Inspect windows for damage (rot, mold, staining), glass condition, putty, paint, weatherstripping, and hardware. Use a **leak detector** to check for air leakage around the frame and movable joints. Some windows may only need minor air sealing, while others require major upgrades or replacement.

### 8.1.7 Condensation problems

Interior surface condensation and frosting are common. New, tighter windows can sometimes worsen the problem by increasing indoor humidity. Solutions include:

-   Reducing humidity levels and improving house ventilation.
-   Increasing the window's surface temperature by installing more efficient windows (e.g., **ENERGY STAR certified** or triple-glazed).
-   For condensation between panes on non-sealed units: **weatherstrip the inner sash** more tightly than the outer pane to prevent moist air ingress. Ensure weep holes are open.
-   For condensation inside a sealed double-glazed unit (due to a broken seal): **replace the glazing unit** (check warranty).

### 8.1.8 Interior caulking

Apply a continuous bead of caulk around the window trim where it meets the wall, at mitred joints, and between the trim and frame. Use interior, paintable, good-quality caulk. For larger gaps (6 mm or less), insulate the gap before caulking. For larger gaps, use a backer rod with caulking or low-expansion foam. Red technical tape can be used for wall-window frame joints, ensuring it's hidden by trim.

### 8.1.9 Exterior caulking

Exterior caulking is a secondary defense against rain. **Properly applied flashing and a detailed drainage plane are the primary defenses**. Exterior caulking should only be done *after* interior sealing is complete to avoid trapping warm, moist air within the wall, which can cause damage.

### 8.1.10 Weatherstripping

Weatherstripping creates a seal between the frame and door/window sash to block air leakage, rain, and dust. Common types include compression, brush, sweep, and magnetic. **Compression or magnetic seals** are typically the most airtight (found on casement, awning, hopper, and tilt-turn windows).

*   *Technical note*: Building codes require at least one egress window in every bedroom for emergency escape.
*   **Preparation and installation**: Adjust/square windows, remove old weatherstripping/caulking/paint, clean surfaces, apply new weatherstripping (reinforce adhesive types with staples if used often), check for smooth operation, and periodically inspect for wear.
*   **Double-hung and single-hung windows**: Use V-type weatherstripping on sides, top, and bottom of moving sash. Caulk fixed portions.
*   **Sliding windows**: Typically use brush weatherstripping. Sashless sliders are inefficient; supplement with interior storm windows or replace with energy-efficient units.
*   **Swing-type (hinged) windows**: Treat like doors. Apply weatherstripping to the frame or stop. Often require a combination of V-strip and compression-type weatherstripping.

### 8.1.11 Storm window systems

Storm windows or double-glazed sealed units can **reduce heat loss by almost half** for single-glazed windows.

-   **Exterior storm window systems**: Can be seasonal (installed in fall, removed in spring) or permanent. The main interior window must be air sealed more tightly than the storm window to prevent moisture issues.
-   **Interior storm window systems**: Attached directly to the window frame, reducing air leakage. Must be sealed tightly to minimize condensation. Lighter and more accessible than exterior storm windows, especially on upper floors.
    -   **Heat-shrink film with double-sided tape**: Inexpensive, temporary, DIY kits. Provides excellent seal and visibility but can lift paint upon removal, and the window cannot be opened.
    -   **Clear rigid acrylic sheets with magnetic seals**: More durable and expensive. Requires professional installation. Easier to clean and has a finished appearance. Must be stored properly.
-   **Specialty products**:
    -   **Applied window films (clear or tinted polyester) and multi-layered polyester films (black/silver)**: Reduce solar gain and protect furnishings. **Caution**: May increase heating bills if used on all windows, carry a small risk of glass breakage, and might void original window warranty.
    -   **Curtains, blinds, shutters, shades, awnings, and trees**: Help reduce radiant heat loss in winter and solar gain in summer. Insulated drapes offer more benefit. Non-airtight coverings may cause winter condensation.

### 8.1.12 Skylights

Skylights should have a **low curb** to reduce heat loss through the frame. Common issues are water leakage and condensation around the frame, curb, or tunnel. For operable skylights, ensure hardware and seals are in good order. Curbs and interior tunnels must be **well insulated and air sealed** to reduce condensation. Light-reflective glazing and blinds can reduce summer overheating. Avoid opening skylights in hot weather if the house air is cooler, as this can cause a **reverse stack effect**, drawing hot air into the home and reducing cooling effectiveness.

## 8.2 Doors

Doors come in swinging (hinged) and sliding styles. They can be solid or have glazing (doorlite, sidelite, transom). **Insulated core doors are more thermally resistant** than solid material doors or those with extensive glazing. Poor installation, wear, shifting foundations, and warping can lead to air leakage.

### 8.2.1 Weatherstripping the doors

-   **Top and sides**: Use good quality **V-shaped vinyl weatherstripping** or combination metal/foam/rubber types. Adjust regularly.
-   **Bottom**: Apply weatherstripping to the door sill (threshold) or the door itself. Use durable, flexible, and replaceable materials that can withstand traffic. Gasketed door-bottom weatherstripping (on the door) or full/partial threshold weatherstripping (on the sill) are effective.
-   **Sliding patio doors**: Replace worn weatherstripping and hardware. Replace poor condition units with **ENERGY STAR certified units**. Temporarily seal unused winter patio doors with removable sealant or heat-shrink film.

## 8.3 Contracting the work

-   **Air sealing and repairing** windows and doors can be part of an air sealing contractor's work. Ask your contractor to check and replace defective weatherstripping on remaining units.
-   When getting estimates, ensure the contractor has relevant experience and offers a **third-party warranty** on products and services.
-   **Professional installation** is crucial to ensure product warranty coverage and proper operation. Poor installation can lead to drafts and costly water leaks.
-   **Installation criteria**:
    -   Provide an **airtight, insulated seal**.
    -   Window/door should retain original shape, be level and square.
    -   Should not support external loads unless specifically designed for it.
    -   Exterior finishing (e.g., flashing) must prevent water penetration.
    -   Appropriate sealing tapes and caulking should be used as recommended by the manufacturer.
    -   Skylights/tunnels should be well sealed and insulated.
-   **Installation types**:
    -   **Insert retrofit**: New unit installed into the existing frame. Less expensive, less disturbance. Only suitable if the existing frame is in good condition, properly sealed, and insulated. Not typical for skylights.
    -   **Complete tear out**: Removal of the old unit, including the frame. Allows for an airtight, insulated seal with the rough opening. The original opening can be retained or enlarged.

*For certified window installations, refer to the **Window Wise program**.* Further information on energy-efficient windows, doors, and skylights is available on the Government of Canada website.
