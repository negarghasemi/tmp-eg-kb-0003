# Energy efficiency housing initiatives

## EnerGuide Rating System

Natural Resources Canada introduced the home energy rating system in **1998** to provide an unbiased, credible way to estimate how energy is used in a home and encourage measures to reduce energy use. It became a standard across Canada, adopted by other jurisdictions and utilities for their home energy programs. Since its introduction, it has been used to estimate the energy efficiency of more than **1 million homes**.

### Purpose and Benefits

The EnerGuide Rating System estimates the energy performance of a house and can be used for both **existing homes** and in the **planning phase for new construction**. It provides building professionals with information to help consumers with home purchase decisions and to choose the best renovations to maximize savings on energy bills.

### Process

**Energy advisors** who work with licensed service organizations perform EnerGuide home evaluations. This helps homeowners make informed decisions about the most effective energy efficiency renovations. Builders can also work with energy advisors in the planning phase to evaluate plans and determine cost-effective upgrades to increase energy efficiency, resulting in savings for homebuyers.

### Updates

In **2011 and 2012**, Natural Resources Canada conducted comprehensive consultations with over 300 partners and industry representatives. Stakeholders requested updates to the rating system, including the label, to make it simpler to understand, integrate new technologies, and be more reflective of actual energy use.

## ENERGY STAR for New Homes

The international ENERGY STAR program was developed by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in **1992**. Natural Resources Canada's Office of Energy Efficiency (OEE) has promoted the ENERGY STAR symbol in Canada and monitored its use since **2001**. The program encourages energy-efficient practices to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and helps Canadians lower energy bills and environmental impact.

### Scope

The ENERGY STAR symbol qualifies a wide range of products including major appliances, heating and cooling equipment, lighting, office equipment, and home electronics. In **2005**, energy-efficient new homes were added to the list of certified products in Canada.

### Standard and Performance

In **2012**, Natural Resources Canada published the **ENERGY STAR for New Homes Standard Version 12**. Homes built to this Standard are, on average, **20% more energy-efficient than typical new homes**.

### Builder Options

Builders can meet the Standard through two paths:

-   **Performance Path**: Involves energy modeling using NRCans energy simulation software to confirm performance. Builders may also acquire an EnerGuide label in addition to the ENERGY STAR for New Homes certified label and certificate.
-   **Prescriptive Path**: Includes several predetermined builder option packages based on provincial location. This option is beneficial for tract builders who construct numerous homes and want to build to the ENERGY STAR Standard or offer energy efficiency upgrade packages. Upon verification, a certified label and certificate are issued for each home.

## R-2000 Homes

R-2000 is an industry-endorsed technical performance standard for **energy efficiency, indoor air tightness quality, and environmental responsibility** in new home construction.

### History

-   **1970s**: Energy crisis spurred a movement toward energy efficiency.
-   **1978**: The Saskatchewan Demonstration House was built, showcasing innovative design, modern technology, good building practices, and materials (passive solar, additional insulation, air sealing).
-   **1980**: The federal government established the Super Energy Efficient Home (SEEH) Program to stimulate construction of energy-efficient houses.
-   **1984**: Natural Resources Canada (then Energy and Mines and Resources Canada) provided training and incentives for builders to construct R-2000 certified homes.
-   **1982**: The R-2000 Program was officially launched, based on technical guidelines exceeding building codes, a computer-based energy analysis tool, a network of trained professionals, and industry collaboration.

### Impact and Updates

The R-2000 Standard has significantly transformed the energy performance of the new housing market since its introduction, becoming the benchmark for energy-efficient new home building in Canada. It paved the way for innovative energy efficiency building techniques and created a market for technologies like heat recovery ventilators and high-efficiency furnaces and windows. The R-2000 Standard has been continuously developed and updated by NRCan in coordination with Canadian home building professionals and other key industry stakeholders. The updated R-2000 Standard was published in **2012**.

### R-2000 Homes Features

-   Homes built to the Standard are on average **50% more energy-efficient than typical new homes**.
-   They include **clean air features** that exceed building code requirements, such as:
    -   Hardwood flooring
    -   Low-emission cabinetry
    -   Low-emission (low volatile organic compound [VOC]) paints
    -   Non-solvent-based adhesives and finishes
-   A **whole-house mechanical ventilation system** is professionally installed in every R-2000 home.
-   Additional features contributing to environmental stewardship include:
    -   Materials made with recycled content
    -   Energy savings devices
    -   Water conservation measures
