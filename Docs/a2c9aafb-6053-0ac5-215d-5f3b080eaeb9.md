# Keeping The Heat In - Section 9: Operating your house: thermostats, water heaters, furnaces and other heating/cooling systems

*Like any system, your house will run only as efficiently as you operate and maintain it. Operating it efficiently will maximize your retrofit gains and can actually improve your home's heating, cooling and ventilation performance and overall durability. Even more important, you will create a healthier, more comfortable living environment. As part of your house as a system, a well-tuned and efficiently operating heating, ventilating and air-conditioning (HVAC) system can significantly reduce your annual energy bill.*

## 9.1 Operating and maintaining the heating, ventilating and air-conditioning system

Follow the manufacturers recommended maintenance procedures for cleaning and servicing heating and cooling equipment.

- **Oil-fired furnaces and boilers** require **annual cleaning and tuning**.
- **Natural gas and propane furnaces and boilers** and **ground-source heat pumps** should be serviced at least **every two years**.
- **Solid fuel burning equipment** (e.g., wood stove or fireplace) and chimneys should be **inspected annually and cleaned** as often as necessary.

### 9.1.1 Homeowner maintenance

*A qualified service technician should perform annual maintenance and efficiency tune-ups, but homeowners can do some of the work.*

> #### Safety warning:
> Building codes now require that every home with a combustion appliance or attached garage must have a **carbon monoxide (CO) detector**. One should be located near the furnace/appliance or within 5 m (15 ft.) of each bedroom door to ensure the alarm can be heard. Follow installation and operation instructions. Check with your building or fire department for more information.

- **Forced-air system**: Keep return-air grilles and warm-air vents clean and free of obstructions. **Change or clean filters every three months** or as they become loaded.
- **Electric or hydronic baseboard systems**: **Vacuum radiators each autumn** to prevent dust buildup. Gently straighten bent heating fins with a plastic comb to improve efficiency.
- **Hydronic systems**: Perform best when radiators are relatively free of air bubbles and operating at correct pressures. **Bleed radiators regularly** unless the system has automatic bleeding. Automatically achieve further savings with an **outdoor reset control** that adjusts the operating temperature of the circulating water according to outdoor temperatures.

### 9.1.2 Thermostats

*You can save energy by turning down your thermostat, and the best way to do this is to install a programmable setback thermostat.*

- A basic programmable thermostat provides a clock timer and allows at least two setback and reset periods a day.
    - **Example**: Program a temperature reduction before bedtime, ending before morning. A second setback can reduce temperature when away during the day, ending before evening arrival.
- **Energy Savings**: A drop of **1C (2F) over an eight-hour period can save about 2 percent** on heating energy consumption.
- **When away from home**: If away for more than three or four hours, it is worthwhile to turn down the temperature.
- **Moisture Risk**: Do not reduce the temperature lower than **17C (63F)**, as there is a risk of moisture build-up in exterior walls.
- **Extended Absences**: If away for extended periods (a week or longer), **keep humidity levels low** (refer to Part 2.4, *Control of moisture flow*).
- **Monitoring Humidity**: Measure relative humidity with a hygrometer. If levels are not low, reduce humidity by controlling moisture sources or adding an **ENERGY STAR certified dehumidifier**.
- **Smart Thermostats**: Allow remote control of heating/cooling via smartphone, computer, or tablet. May provide energy use information for tracking.
- **Adaptive Thermostats**: A subset of smart thermostats that monitor weather, occupancy, or proximity to establish an accurate schedule. They adapt heating and cooling to needs, offering greater savings than programmable thermostats.

### 9.1.3 Sizing and balancing an HVAC system

- **Newly retrofitted house**: Will have reduced heat loss and require a smaller heating/cooling system. Existing fuel-fired heating equipment may now be **oversized**.
    - **Oversizing issues**: Can cause larger temperature fluctuations and inefficient short cycling, especially for mid-efficiency furnaces and boilers. Affects occupant comfort and equipment wear.
    - **Chimney concerns**: For mid-efficiency units, less frequent operation can make the chimney colder, increasing potential for down drafting, condensation, and damage. If retrofits are extensive, have the system checked by a qualified technician.
- **Solution for short cycling**: Consider replacing the furnace with a **high-efficiency 2-stage, 3-stage, or fully modulating unit**.
    - **Modulating boilers** are also available with outdoor reset controls to improve energy efficiency by reducing water supply temperature.
    - **Staged/Modulating units**: Run efficiently at lower firing rates in milder weather (e.g., 60% capacity). Provides consistent lower heating output over longer periods, increasing comfort and reducing short cycling.
    - **Improved Air Quality**: Operating at steadier, lower capacity allows more air through the filter, leading to better indoor air quality.
- **Heat distribution system balancing**: A persistent problem in all homes. **Undersized/leaking ductwork, inadequate return air grilles** can cause discomfort and higher bills.
    - **Rebalancing**: Adjusting dampers in ducted systems or valves in hot water systems may help. Otherwise, hire a competent contractor.
    - **Duct cleaning**: Alone will not resolve balancing issues and has little effect on HVAC system or indoor air quality.

### 9.1.4 Motor Selection

*The type of fan or pump motor(s) in HVAC equipment (furnace, boiler, HRV/ERV) impacts overall efficiency.*

- **Permanent Split Capacitor (PSC) motors**:
    - Typically **60% to 65% efficiency** at peak capacity; less efficient at lower speeds.
    - Tend to start and stop abruptly; limited speed change options with little energy savings.
    - Can get very hot, a disadvantage if used for cooling/AC.
- **Direct Current Brushless Motors (ECM)**:
    - Can be programmed for a much **greater range of speeds**.
    - Start and run quietly.
    - Efficiency stays high (near **80%**) even if speed is reduced.
    - Operate much cooler.
    - Can meet required airflow even with dust filters and dirty AC coils causing restrictions.
- **Important Note**: ECM motors consume more power to overcome duct system resistance (e.g., dirty filter, restrictive ductwork). **Good duct design, wise filter selection, and regular cleaning/filter changes** contribute to an efficient system.

### 9.1.5 Air distribution ducts

- **Sealing**: Seal all plenums and supply/return ducts with **aluminum foil duct tape, approved flexible plastic tape, or water-based mastic** to improve comfort, safety, and system balancing.
- **Insulation**: Heating ducts running through unheated or cool basements and crawl spaces should be insulated.
    - Use **RSI 2.1 (R-12) or more of batt or blanket insulation** or specially designed foil-faced fiberglass blanket insulation.
    - Wrap insulation around ducts and secure with string, zip ties, or approved tape. **Do not use standard vinyl duct tape**.
- **Wood-fired furnaces**: Do not seal or wrap ducts within **1.8 m (6 ft.)** of a wood-fired furnace unless using special, approved non-combustible tape, mastic, or insulation.
- **Internal duct sealing**: An option is to seal ducts from the interior using a **blown-in plastic polymer** for joints, gaps, and holes 10 mm (3/8 in.) or smaller. This is done by a qualified technician.
- **Hydronic systems**: Place **foil-covered insulation board** between radiators and exterior walls to reflect heat. Insulate metallic and plastic water lines with a minimum of **RSI 0.7 (R-4) approved insulation**.
- **Boiler insulation**: Insulated jackets are available for some boilers to offer efficiency gains in cold areas of the house.

### 9.1.6 Open hearth fireplaces

- **Efficiency**: Open hearth gas- or wood-fired fireplaces are mainly decorative and generally do not provide net heat gain due to heat loss up the chimney.
- **Accessories**: Tightly fitted glass doors offer only nominal improvements.
- **Modern Alternatives**: The hearth industry offers a variety of efficient **direct vent zero-clearance fireplaces and inserts** (gas, propane, pellet, wood units) that eliminate standby losses.
- **Air Leakage Reduction**: Refer to Part 4.2.5, *Tips on sealing some of the leakiest areas - Fireplaces* for tips on reducing air leakage from unused fireplaces.

## 9.2 Domestic hot water

- **Energy Consumption**: Domestic water heaters (DWHs) consume an average of **20 percent of a homes energy**  similar to all lights and appliances combined.
- **Largest Energy User**: Next to space heating, the DWH is typically the largest energy user. In new energy-efficient homes, the DWH load can be greater than the space heating load.

### 9.2.1 Replacing your fuel-fired DWH system

- **Best Option**: Replace existing systems with a **new high-efficiency system**, including tank-type, instantaneous, or combination space and water heating models.
- **Sizing**: Match the size of the heater to your needs. Bigger tank-type units are typically less efficient if hot water demand is low. Use manufacturer/retailer sizing charts.
- **On-demand water heaters**: May increase wait times for hot water. Discuss with your plumber how to reduce wait time and wasted water.
- **Energy-efficient models**: Look for **ENERGY STAR certified DWH units** and **solar hot water heating systems**.

#### Energy saving tips for DWH systems

- **Reduce hot water use**:
    - Fix leaks and drips.
    - Wash clothes in cold water.
    - Use low-flow showerheads and restrictive aerators on faucets.
- **Location**: Locate the water heater close to the point-of-use or use small-diameter piping directly from the tank to each point-of-use.
- **Recirculation**: If point-of-use is **9 m (30 ft.) or more** from the water heater, install a **demand-type hot water recirculation system** (with or without a return line).
- **Insulation (Electric Tank)**: Install an insulated base and an insulating kit around the tank for electric tank-type water heaters in basements.
- **Pipe Insulation**: Insulate both metallic and plastic water lines with a minimum of **RSI 0.7 (R-4) compatible insulation**.
- **Drain Water Heat Recovery**: Install a drain water heat recovery unit on the main vertical or horizontal stack(s) serving showers.
- **Solar Water Heater**: Install a solar water heater to pre-heat water for the DWH system.

## 9.3 Cooling systems

*Retrofitting reduces energy needed to cool your home. Air conditioning lowers both air temperature and humidity levels.*

- **Oversizing**: An oversized AC system lowers temperature too quickly without removing excess humidity, leading to a cool, damp house, which can promote mold.
- **Comfort and Savings**: Higher indoor temperatures (e.g., **26C/79F**) with reduced humidity are more comfortable and save energy.
- **Maintenance**: Air conditioners should be serviced and maintained regularly. Inefficiency occurs with dirty inside coils, blocked outdoor condenser airways, or low refrigerant levels.
    - **Homeowner tasks**: Clean or change the air filter, keep the outside condenser free from obstructions.
    - **Professional service**: A service contractor should periodically maintain your unit. Check the owners manual for maintenance information.

### 9.3.1. Balancing an air conditioning system

- **Multi-story homes**: Upper levels are often hotter due to solar heat gains and warm air rising. Central systems typically have one thermostat on the main floor, leading to temperature imbalances.
- **Manual Adjustments**: Partially close dampers in the basement and main floor and leave upper-level dampers fully open to balance air distribution. Dampers can be added by a technician if not present. Individual registers can also be adjusted but are less effective.
- **Monitoring**: Use thermometers on different levels to assist with manual adjustments.
- **Duct Leakage**: If balancing attempts are ineffective, ductwork may be too leaky, causing air to escape rather than reaching intended rooms.
- **Insufficient Ducting**: If return air or supply ducts are inadequate, balancing may not work.
    - **Solutions**: Install a separate **ductless mini-split air conditioner** on upper floors. Alternatively, install a large duct from the basement to the upper floor to return hot stale air to the main return ductwork.
- **Safety Warning**: **Mark damper positions before adjusting**. Do not restrict air supply back to the furnace, as this can cause AC coil freezing or trigger the furnaces high heat limit switch.
- **Professional Consultation**: Discuss concerns with your heating and cooling technician. Other solutions may include separate heating/cooling systems per floor.

### 9.3.2. Portable air conditioners

- **Efficiency**: Portable air conditioners (PACs) are generally not very efficient because the entire unit is indoors.
- **Single-hose PACs**: **Avoid these** as they are least effective. They create negative pressure, drawing in hot, humid outdoor air, leading to prolonged operation, higher energy bills, and noise.
- **Dual-hose PACs**: A better alternative. They cool space quickly without exhausting indoor air. An additional hose draws outside air, which passes through the compressor and coil, capturing heat and exhausting it outside. This prevents negative pressure and increases cooling capability.

## 9.4 Ventilation and combustion air

- **Fresh Air Needs**: People need fresh ventilation air for indoor air quality. Fuel-fired systems need combustion air.
- **Air Leakage**: Most Canadian homes have too much excess air due to poorly sealed building envelopes, causing about **25 percent of heat loss**.
- **Better Approach**: Install a **ventilation system** capable of changing total household air once every three hours, plus providing separate combustion air for fuel-burning appliances.
- **Moisture Balance**: Systematically assess moisture sources, problem symptoms, and ventilation requirements.
    - If signs of excessive condensation exist, reduce moisture sources. If not possible, any air-tightening retrofit must include a **mechanical ventilation system**.
- **Ventilation Systems**: Some systems exhaust stale air, some supply fresh air, and some do both (balanced).
- **Benefits of Balanced Ventilation (with Heat Recovery)**: Controls ventilation rate, maximizes air tightening, increases home durability, and improves indoor air quality with positive health effects.

### 9.4.1 Is your house susceptible to indoor air quality problems?

- **Factors increasing susceptibility**:
    - Houses without a conventional chimney and/or circulating air duct system.
    - Competition for air from fireplaces and/or powerful exhaust vents (e.g., kitchen range hoods).
    - Non-ducted range hoods or undersized/inoperable bathroom fans.
    - Air sealing a home without adequate ventilation.
    - Sources of air contamination (smokers, burning candles, hobbies, etc.).
    - High humidity levels.
    - High radon levels (refer to Part 1.4, *Health and safety considerations*).

### 9.4.2 Some signs of indoor air quality and moisture problems

- **Indicators of problems**:
    - Excessive condensation on double-paned windows.
    - Staining and mold growth (often in bathrooms, closets, exterior walls).
    - Stuffy, musty atmosphere and lingering odors.
    - Back-puffing and odors from space and water heating equipment.
    - Backdrafts of combustion gases or odors from the fireplace.

### 9.4.3 Solutions to moisture problems

- **First step: Reduce water vapor**:
    - Do not store firewood indoors.
    - Avoid drying laundry indoors.
    - Vent clothes dryer to the exterior.
    - Disconnect humidifiers.
    - Cover exposed earth floors in basements/crawlspaces with a moisture barrier.
    - Install a sump pump with a cover.
    - Fix all water leaks into the basement.
    - Do not allow standing water in the house or against the foundation wall.
    - Ensure ground slopes away from the foundation and eavestroughs function properly.
    - Operate kitchen and bathroom fans during use.
    - Adjust living habits to produce less humidity (cleaning, washing, houseplants, aquariums).

- **Indicators**: Excessive condensation or frosting on double-glazed windows (except kitchen/bathroom) indicates too much moisture. A hygrometer can also monitor humidity.

### 9.4.4 Increasing ventilation

- **When to increase ventilation**: If condensation persists after reducing moisture production, or if indoor air quality is poor.
- **Ventilation Categories**: Balanced and unbalanced.
    - **Unbalanced**: Most common, uses exhaust fans, relies on air leakage for replacement air. Can cause reduced house pressures and ineffective ventilation.
    - **Balanced**: Replaces exhausted air with a dedicated source of incoming air, keeping house pressures neutral and providing more even ventilation.
- **Methods to increase ventilation**:
    - Turn on kitchen and bathroom fans during use. Use a timer or humidity controller for automatic operation.
    - Install **ENERGY STAR certified fans**. Choose quieter models for continuous use.
    - Ensure all fans exhaust fully to the exterior and incorporate air sealing. Avoid recirculating kitchen range hoods.
    - Install a balanced central system with a **heat or energy recovery ventilator (HRV or ERV)** for improved indoor air quality.
- **Fresh-air duct to forced-air system**: A somewhat effective technique involves installing a fresh-air duct with a damper to the return-air plenum of a forced-air system.
    - The forced air system should be interlocked with exhaust fans to avoid pressurizing the house.
    - Inlet should be a minimum of **3 m (10 ft.)** from the furnace inlet.
    - Open the damper just enough to prevent window condensation, adjusting seasonally. A motorized damper with a humidistat can automate this.
- **Central exhaust systems**: Some systems use a central exhaust fan with ducts pulling air from kitchens and bathrooms. Professionals should design and install these.

### 9.4.5 Heat recovery ventilators and energy recovery ventilators

- **HRV/ERV**: One of the best ways to ventilate a home and control indoor air quality. Connected to existing forced-air or dedicated ductwork systems in a balanced manner.
- **Balance**: Supply and exhaust air flows must be balanced for maximum performance and to avoid affecting house pressure.
- **Motors**: Look for models with **electronically commutated motors (ECM)** to reduce power consumption.
- **Controls**: Available with various controls, including programmable units.
- **HRV Function**: Saves energy by recovering heat from exhausted air. Heat exchanger transfers heat to fresh incoming air.
- **ERV Function**: Similar to HRV, but also transfers moisture between air streams. Recommended for high cooling loads, very low interior moisture loads, or dry climates.
    - **Cooling Season**: ERVs help control excess moisture. In hot, humid regions, moisture from outdoor supply air is transferred to outgoing exhaust air, reducing AC load.
    - **Heating Season**: ERVs can help recover moisture from exhausted air, maintaining indoor humidity and minimizing static electricity and discomfort from dry air. *Note: Dryness could indicate over-ventilation.*
- **Installation & Maintenance**: Must be installed, balanced, and commissioned properly by a certified technician. Homeowners can perform routine maintenance (cleaning filters/core, checking components).

### 99.4.6 Combustion air

- **Appliance Needs**: All fuel-burning appliances require air for combustion and to dilute/exhaust combustion products.
- **Negative Pressure Risk**: If air supplied is less than air exhausted, negative pressure is created, causing **backdrafts of combustion gases**.
    - **Example**: Powerful kitchen fan or open fireplace exhausts air, pulling combustion gases back into the house through chimneys/vents.
- **Signs of combustion air problems**:
    - Back-puffing of furnace, boiler, or water heater (soot/staining, melted plastic fittings).
    - Unusual odors or hot, muggy air around combustion appliance.
    - Difficulty starting or maintaining a fire in the fireplace.
    - Frequent headaches, skin/throat irritations, nausea in occupants.
    - Unexplained or nuisance smoke/carbon monoxide alarm signals.
- **First Line of Defense**: Replace spillage-susceptible heating and DWH equipment with **direct vent or sealed combustion appliances**. Alternatively, select electric heating systems with heat pump technology.
- **Conventional Fireplaces**: Can be problematic, leaking heated air and prone to backdrafting. Minimize problems by installing **tight-fitting glass doors**, opening a nearby window slightly during use, or installing a **designated combustion air supply**.

> ### Safety warning:
> - **CO detectors**: Always install CO detectors in homes with combustion appliances or attached garages. Replace batteries regularly. These devices have limited lifespans and must be replaced per manufacturer's instructions.
> - **Vehicles**: Never operate a vehicle in an enclosed space. Always open the garage door before starting a car inside.
