## Chapter 1: A Special Place in the Home

Modern homes are incompatible with conventional wood-burning fireplaces due to increased insulation and airtightness, leading to inefficiency and harmful emissions. Energy-efficient gas fireplaces offer a safe, energy-efficient, and environmentally friendly alternative.

**Benefits of Gas Fireplaces:**
- Convenience of an on/off switch and ever-present fuel supply.
- Cleanliness (no ashes, wood chips, bark).
- Elimination of chimney cleaning.
- Safety of sealed combustion units.
- Environmental benefits compared to conventional wood fireplaces.

*Not all gas fireplaces are created equal; some are very efficient, others are not. This booklet helps consumers choose wisely.*

## Chapter 2: Gas Fireplaces and the Environment

Gas fireplaces are known for clean-burning characteristics, producing much less carbon monoxide and particulate emissions than wood fireplaces. However, they still release pollutants like nitrogen oxides and carbon dioxide. Buying an energy-efficient gas fireplace and using it wisely will reduce energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions.

## Chapter 3: What to Look For

### Types of Gas Fireplaces
- **Inserts**: Convert existing wood-burning fireplaces to gas. Require relining existing chimneys with approved vents.
- **Zero-clearance gas fireplaces**: For new installations, can be installed inside the house envelope. Require careful adherence to manufacturer's instructions for clearances and venting.
- **Free-standing gas fireplaces**: Resemble wood-burning stoves, often more effective at heating as all surfaces are exposed to the room.

### Types of Venting
- **Natural draft venting**: Uses a vertical chimney (B-vent or approved metal liner) and a draft hood. Requires extra house air.
- **Power venting**: Uses an electrical fan to assist venting, allowing for more flexible flue locations and less house air use.
- **Direct-vent fireplace**: Draws outdoor combustion air directly into the firebox and exhausts products outdoors through a separate pipe. Units are sealed, requiring no room air for combustion, and prevent loss of heated room air. Typically installed on outside walls.
    - **Coaxial venting**: Uses two concentric pipes (outer for intake, inner for exhaust). Common for sidewall venting.
    - **Collinear venting**: Uses two separate pipes (one for intake, one for exhaust). Used when space is limited or for long vent distances/cold temperatures.

*To be installed in an energy-efficient R-2000 home, a gas fireplace must be either direct-vented (sealed) or power-vented to provide protection against spillage and increase efficiency.*

### Efficiency Ratings
- Many older efficiency ratings were **steady-state measurements**, which do not reflect actual operating efficiency in a home.
- The best way to determine efficiency is through the **EnerGuide Fireplace Efficiency (FE) rating** based on the **CSA-P.4 test method**.

### The New EnerGuide Fireplace Efficiency (FE) Rating
- Established in **September 2003** by the Government of Canada and HRAI.
- Provides a standardized method for testing and comparing gas fireplaces.
- Assesses all gas fireplaces (decorative or space heating).
- Reflects overall operation throughout the heating season.
- Expressed as a **percentage**; higher percentage means higher efficiency.
- Appears on the **EnerGuide label**.

### The EnerGuide Label for Gas Fireplaces
- Shows the **FE rating** (percentage).
- States: \"Based on CSA P.4.1-02\" to confirm testing method.

### Energy Efficiency Considerations
- All models have an EnerGuide rating.
- Vented gas fireplaces can be both attractive and energy efficient.
- Higher FE numbers indicate better efficiency.
- Actual savings depend on location, fireplace efficiency, and house efficiency.
- Differentiate between \"decorative\" and \"heater\" appliance ratings.

### Input and Output Ratings
- **Input rating**: Fuel energy consumed per hour (Btu/h or GJ/h).
- **Output rating**: Heat supplied to the house (Btu/h or GJ/h).
- **Key Point**: High input does not guarantee high output. Output depends on input and efficiency.
- **Example**: A **20,000 Btu/h fireplace at 70% efficiency** provides the same heat as a **40,000 Btu/h unit at 35% efficiency**, but uses half the fuel.
- **Recommendation**: Focus on output rating for proper sizing. Avoid oversizing to prevent overheating and wasted fuel. Look for models with **lower output** or features to **\"dial down\" input** (modulation/turndown) or **move heat to other rooms**.

### Electronic or Intermittent Ignition vs. Continuous Pilot Lights
- **Continuous pilot light**: Consumes **600 to 1500 Btu of gas per hour**. If left on, significantly increases annual energy costs.
- **Recommendation**: Turn off the pilot light when the fireplace is not in frequent use (summer, or during heating season if rarely used).
- **Electronic/intermittent ignition**: Eliminates the need for a continuous pilot, saving energy and money.
- *Note*: Some prefer continuous pilot for auxiliary heat during power outages. If so, choose a unit with easy pilot shut-off and relighting.

### Variable-Setting Controls and Thermostats
- **Variable-setting control (turndown)**: Adjusts heat output by regulating gas consumption. Allows for better comfort, prevents overheating, and maintains flame view. Reduces energy loss from frequent cycling.
- **Recommendation**: Look for a **wide turndown range** (e.g., down to 20% of full load, not just 70%).
- **Ductwork**: Some fireplaces can connect to ductwork to distribute heat to other areas, preventing overheating in the immediate room.
- **Automatic thermostat controls**: Adjust firing rate to maintain constant room temperature. Effective on fireplaces with wide turndown ranges. On limited turndown units, can cause frequent on/off cycling, wasting energy and affecting flame enjoyment.

### Heat Exchange and Transfer
- **Radiation**: Transfer of heat from flames and hot surfaces to objects. Radiant heat makes people feel warm even if air is cool.
- **Convection**: Circulation of room air picks up heat from fireplace surfaces. Well-designed fireplaces have channels for natural convection.
- **Heat exchanger**: Extracts more heat from combustion gases and transfers it to the house (secondary heat exchange).
- **Variable-speed fireplace fans**: Increase convective heat distribution and air circulation. Look for quiet fans.
- **Ceiling fan**: Improves circulation of heated air from a fireplace.
- **Surface area and exposure**: Free-standing and hearth-mounted fireplaces with more exposed surfaces tend to be more efficient.

## Chapter 4: Locating a Fireplace for Maximum Benefit

- **Strategic Placement**: Install in a major living area where heat can access other parts of the house (e.g., family room, dining room, living room, kitchen on the main floor).
- **Layout**: Open designs with few walls and accessible stairwells allow heat to move effectively.
- **Zoning**: Efficient gas fireplaces can lower heating costs in homes with electric baseboards by providing comfort heat, while baseboards maintain temperatures in remote rooms.
- **Exterior Walls**: If installing against an outside wall, build the fireplace inside the house envelope or choose a unit with an insulated outer casing to prevent heat loss.
- **Interior Walls**: Locating the fireplace and chimney on an inside wall surrounds the vent with warm air, ensuring better draft and reducing negative pressure issues.
- **Basement Installation**: Direct-vent units are recommended as they don't require a chimney and are sealed. Consider insulating the basement first. Choose a low-input fireplace or one with ducts to transfer heat.

**The House as a Chimney:**
- In winter, a house acts as a warm, buoyant column of air, creating an upward draft (higher pressure at top, lower at bottom).
- If an unsealed (non-direct-vent) gas fireplace is installed in a home with lower indoor pressure than outside, the house can become a more effective chimney than the fireplace's own chimney.
- This can lead to **reverse flow**, drawing combustion gases (e.g., carbon monoxide) back into the living area, creating hazardous indoor air quality problems.

## Chapter 5: Summary of Features to Look for in an Energy-Efficient Gas Fireplace

When choosing a gas fireplace, prioritize models with high EnerGuide Fireplace Efficiency (FE) ratings that match your heating needs. Look for:

- A **high EnerGuide Fireplace Efficiency (FE) rating** (as tested to CSA P.4.1-02).
- **Direct-vent design** (where appropriate).
- An **intermittent electronic ignition system** or easy pilot light shut-off/relighting.
- A **ceramic glass front**.
- A **quiet squirrel-cage-type circulating fan** for convective heat transfer.
- A **secondary heat exchanger**.
- An **insulated outer casing** (if on an exterior wall).
- **Good turndown capability** or other means (like ducting) to prevent localized overheating.

## Chapter 6: Gas \"Logs\" Not Recommended

- **Ineffective Heating**: Gas logs provide no real heat to the house; they are primarily decorative and waste energy.
- **Pollution Source**: They can be a source of pollutants.
- **Venting Problems**: Wood fireplace chimneys are not designed for the low-temperature, low-flow, high-moisture flue gases of gas logs. This can lead to condensation and chimney deterioration.
- **Draft Issues**: If installed on an outside wall, gas logs may not generate enough heat for a good draft, leading to reverse flow and drawing carbon monoxide into the living area.

## Chapter 7: What to Avoid

- **Vent-free gas fireplaces**: These units release all combustion gases (carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, nitrogen oxides, water vapour) directly into the house. They can cause severe indoor air-quality and moisture problems, especially in airtight homes. **They are not approved for use in Canada.**

## Chapter 8: Installing Your Gas Fireplace

- **Qualified Installation**: Ensure installation by a qualified technician.
- **Permits and Inspections**: A special installation permit is usually required, followed by an inspection. Check with dealers, municipalities, or gas utilities.
- **Insurance**: Inform your insurance company.
- **Estimates**: Obtain several estimates, comparing contractor knowledge, experience, materials, and itemized costs.
- **Contract Details**: Estimates should include:
    - Itemized labor and material costs.
    - Responsibility for permits and fees.
    - Utility and local authority inspections.
    - Scheduling for utility work (e.g., supply pipe installation).
    - Completion timeline.
    - Warranty for materials and labor.
    - Payment schedule and method.
- **References and Reputation**: Ask for homeowner references and check with the Better Business Bureau.
- **Communication**: Request clear explanations from the installer about any aspect of the work.

## Chapter 9: Safety Tips for Operating Your Gas Fireplace

- Do not operate a new fireplace until the area is thoroughly cleaned (drywall dust can harm components).
- Check manufacturer's instructions for mantle/surround modifications (minimum clearances).
- Have the fireplace inspected before initial use and serviced regularly.
- Read manufacturer's instructions carefully before first operation.
- Keep combustibles away from hot surfaces.
- If pilot or flame goes out, wait 5 minutes (or as per instructions) before relighting to clear gas.
- Be alert for unusual odours or flames; contact dealer for servicing. Contact gas company if you smell gas.
- Keep air-circulating passageways and fans clean and unobstructed.
- Wait for the unit to cool completely before cleaning glass with a damp cloth.
- If glass breaks, have a qualified technician replace it with manufacturer-supplied glass.
- For sidewall-exhausting direct-vent units, keep the external vent clear of debris and snow, and maintain minimum clearances.
- If you do not have a sealed direct-vent fireplace, consider installing a **carbon monoxide detector** near the fireplace. If it alarms, open windows, shut off fireplace, leave house, and seek medical attention if symptoms occur. Determine and remedy the source of CO with a professional.

## Chapter 10: Using Your Gas Fireplace Efficiently

- **Pilot Light**: Turn off the pilot light during summer and heating season when not frequently using the fireplace.
- **Thermostat Adjustment**: Turn down your home's main thermostat when the fireplace is operating.
- **Fireplace Thermostat**: If the fireplace has a thermostat, keep it at the lowest acceptable comfort setting. Turn it down when the room is not in use.
- **Glass Cleaning**: Keep the glass clean to maximize radiant heat transfer.

### Step 3: Determine your home's annual heating load

**Formula:**

Annual Heating Load (GJ) = (Heating Bill / 100,000) x (Seasonal Efficiency / Energy Cost/Unit) x Energy Content

**Example:** Annual heating bill $687 (natural gas), gas $0.22/m, conventional gas furnace 60% efficient, natural gas content 37.5 MJ/m.

Annual Heating Load = (687 / 100,000) x (60 / 0.22) x 37.5 = **70 GJ**
