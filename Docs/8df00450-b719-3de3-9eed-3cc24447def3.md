# Swimming Pools and the ENERGY STAR Score in the United States and Canada

## Overview

The ENERGY STAR score assesses a property's energy performance relative to its peers. While stand-alone swimming pools are not eligible for an ENERGY STAR score, adjustments are made for pools that are energy-intensive amenities in other commercial buildings like hotels and schools. The score focuses on the primary use of the building, not the swimming pool itself.

*   **Technical Approach**: An engineered model estimates the energy use for the swimming pool, which is then subtracted from the building's actual energy use. This allows the building to be evaluated as if it had no pool.
*   **Property Types**: Heated swimming pools (indoor or outdoor) are incorporated into the ENERGY STAR score for eligible property types. No adjustments are made for unheated pools due to their significantly lower energy consumption.
*   **Adjustments**: The swimming pool model considers:
    *   **Heating Energy**: For maintaining a constant temperature, accounting for heat loss from convection, evaporation, and radiation.
    *   **Pumping Energy**: For circulating pool water.
*   **Release Date**:
    *   **Most Recent Update**: August 2023
    *   **Original Release**: January 2004

*This document details how the ENERGY STAR score accounts for swimming pools. More information on the overall score development is available in the Technical Reference for the ENERGY STAR Score (PDF, 709 KB).* 

## Theoretical Background

The engineered model for pool energy use is based on the physics of heated pools. Total energy consumed by a heated pool is the sum of pool heating energy and pool pump electrical energy. Heat loss sources include evaporation, convection, long-wave radiation (for outdoor pools), and conduction (ignored due to small contribution). Outdoor pools can also have heat gains from solar irradiation. Pool pump electrical consumption is a function of head loss, pool size, pump efficiency, and circulation time.

**Pool Energy Consumption Equation (Simplified)**:

```
Energy_pool = Energy_heating + Energy_evaporation + Energy_radiation - Solar Irradiation + Energy_pump
```

**Model Assumptions**:

*   **Indoor Pool Heating**: Only evaporation and convection are significant heat loss contributors.
*   **Outdoor Pool Heating**: Evaporation, convection, and radiation are significant heat loss contributors.
*   **Conduction Losses**: Ignored as they are small.
*   **Temperature**: Pools operate at a fixed temperature year-round.
*   **Make-up Water Heating**: Ignored.
*   **Convection**: A fixed convection heat transfer coefficient is used.
*   **Source Energy**: Assumes natural gas for heating and electricity for pumping. Site-to-source energy conversions are applied based on country (U.S. or Canada). *Footnote 1: The pool adjustment was revised in 2018 for U.S. properties, and in 2023 for Canadian properties, to account for updated ratios used in Portfolio Manager to convert site energy to source energy.*
*   **Equation Inputs**: Fixed values are used for most input variables, based on engineering judgments and parametric sensitivity analysis.

## Outdoor Pool

Calculating energy consumption for outdoor pools is more complex due to high variability in input parameters such as wind speed, water and air temperatures, relative humidity, solar radiation, and annual operation time. A parametric sensitivity analysis was conducted across different climates.

**Recommendation**: For outdoor pools, it is recommended to install sub-meters to track energy use and subtract it from the main meter data before entering into Portfolio Manager.

If sub-metering is not possible, Portfolio Manager will apply a conservative estimate for outdoor pool energy consumption, based on the minimum adjustment determined through the parametric sensitivity analysis. This estimate is shown in **Figure 4**.

| Presence of cooking                      | **0 (No)**  |
| Percent of the building that is heated   | **100**     |
| Percent of the building that is cooled   | **100**     |
| HDD (provided by Portfolio Manager)      | **4,937**   |
| CDD (provided by Portfolio Manager)      | **1,046**   |
| Swimming Pool Use Details                | Value       |
| Pool Size                                | **Short Course**|
| Pool Location                            | **Indoor**  |
| Property Type (Set by Portfolio Manager) | **K-12 School**|

### 2. Portfolio Manager computes the actual source EUI

**Computing Actual Source EUI**

| Fuel        | Billings Units | Site kBtu Multiplier | Site kBtu   | Source kBtu Multiplier | Source kBtu    |
| :---------- | :------------- | :------------------- | :---------- | :--------------------- | :------------- |
| Electricity | 800,000 kWh    | 3.412                | 2,729,600   | 2.80                   | 7,642,880      |
| Natural Gas | 30,000 therms  | 100                  | 3,000,000   | 1.05                   | 3,150,000      |
| **Total Source Energy** |                |                      |             |                        | **10,792,880 kBtu**|

*   **Predicted Pool Energy**: For an Indoor, Short Course Pool at a K-12 school in the U.S. = **2,084,866 kBtu** (from Figure 3).
*   **Actual Source Energy (adjusted)** = Total Source Energy - Predicted Pool Energy
    *   10,792,880 kBtu  2,084,866 kBtu = **8,708,014 kBtu Source**
*   **Actual Source EUI** = Actual Source Energy / Total Floor Area
    *   8,708,014 kBtu / 100,000 ft = **87.08 kBtu/ft**

### 3. Portfolio Manager computes the predicted source EUI

*Using the regression model for K-12 schools (refer to www.energystar.gov/ScoreDetails).*

**Computing Predicted Source EUI**

| Variable                 | Actual Building Value | Reference Centering Value | Building Centered Variable | Coefficient | Coefficient * Centered Variable |
| :----------------------- | :-------------------- | :------------------------ | :------------------------- | :---------- | :------------------------------ |
| Constant                 | --                    | --                        | --                         | **101.7**   | **101.7**                       |
| High School              | 1.000                 | --                        | 1.000                      | **14.08**   | **14.08**                       |
| Open Weekends (yes/no)   | 1.000                 | --                        | 1.000                      | **15.66**   | **15.66**                       |
| Number of Workers per 1,000 ft | 0.7000                | 0.7967                    | -0.09670                   | **25.61**   | **-2.476**                      |
| Presence of Cooking (yes/no) | 0.000                 | --                        | 0.000                      | **8.182**   | **0**                           |
| HDD x Percent Heated     | 4,937                 | 3,597                     | 1,340                      | **0.008370**| **11.22**                       |
| CDD x Percent Cooled     | 1,046                 | 1,472                     | -426                       | **0.02059** | **-8.771**                      |
| **Predicted Source EUI (kBtu/ft)** |                       |                           |                            |             | **131.4**                       |

### 4. Portfolio Manager computes the energy efficiency ratio

*   **Ratio** = Actual Source EUI (Step 2) / Predicted Source EUI (Step 3)
    *   Ratio = 87.08 / 131.4 = **0.6627**

### 5. Portfolio Manager uses the efficiency ratio to assign a score via a lookup table

*   A ratio of **0.6627** is greater than 0.6532 and less than 0.6640.
*   **The ENERGY STAR score is 81**.

## Appendix (Summary of Energy Contribution Equations)

**Figure A  1: Energy Contribution from Evaporation Loss**

*   **Rate of Evaporation Loss (Site Energy/ft/hr)**: Depends on wind speed (V), saturation pressures at pool water temperature (P~pw~) and air dew point temperature (P~dp~), and an Activity Factor (AF).
*   **Total Annual Evaporation Loss (Source Energy/yr)**: Calculated using the rate, hours pool is open (t~o~), pool surface area (A~p~), heater efficiency (~h~), and the source-site ratio for gas (S~gas~).

**Figure A  2: Energy Contribution from Convection Loss**

*   **Rate of Convection Loss (Site Energy/ft/hr)**: Depends on convection coefficient (h~c~), pool water temperature (T~w~), and air temperature (T~a~).
*   **Total Annual Convection Loss (Source Energy/yr)**: Calculated using the rate, hours pool is open (t~o~), pool surface area (A~p~), heater efficiency (~h~), and the source-site ratio for gas (S~gas~).

**Figure A  3: Energy Contribution from Radiation Loss**

*   **Rate of Radiation Loss (Site Energy/ft/hr)**: Depends on radiation loss coefficient (h~rad~), pool water temperature (T~w~), and sky temperature (T~s~).
*   **Total Annual Radiation Loss (Source Energy/yr)**: Calculated using the rate, hours pool is open (t~o~), pool surface area (A~p~), heater efficiency (~h~), and the source-site ratio for gas (S~gas~).

**Figure A  4: Energy Contribution from Water Pumping**

*   **Hourly Pumping Energy (Site Energy/hr)**: Depends on head loss (H~Loss~), pool water circulation rate (m dot), and pump overall efficiency (~p~).
*   **Annual Pumping Energy (Source Energy/yr)**: Calculated using hourly pumping energy, pump run time (t~P~), and the source-site ratio for electricity (S~elec~).
